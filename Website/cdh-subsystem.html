★ The Fundamentals

The CDH subsystem acts as the "brain and nervous system" of the spacecraft. It provides command, control, data management, and communication interfaces to every subsystem in some way.

Every CDH subsystem is built from these fundamental capabilities:

Pillar What it does
OBC Hardware Consists of the processors, memory, FPGAs, and storage that execute instructions and store data. Choice of processor achitecture, memory architecture, and FPGA strategy determines performance, power, and reliability.
Flight Software (FSW) Consists of the operating system (OS), applications, drivers, and bootloader to perform logic operations that are routine or commanded. Choice of OS, applications, features, and drivers determine performance, efficiency, and configurability.
Data & Networking Consists of the data buses (SpaceWire, MIL-STD-1553, CAN) and time distribution to communication with every other component. Choice of the data bus architecture and timing determine communication speed and accuracy timed executions.

1. OBC Hardware

The OBC hardware is the physical computing platform of the CDH subsystem — the processors, memory, FPGAs, storage, and interconnects that execute instructions and hold data. Every OBC board must balance performance, power, radiation tolerance, and reliability against the mission's requirements.

CISC Architecture (Complex Instruction Set Computer)

A type of processor capable of complex instruction sets and high code density. The rich set of instructions can perform a variety of tasks, resulting in high code density and efficient use of memory. Known for high performance, used in high degree of parallel processing, and compatible with a wide range of software.

Intel released the 8086 processor in 1978, launching the x86 architecture. The 8086 was a 16-bit CISC processor with 29,000 transistors running at 5–10 MHz. The architecture was later extended to 32-bit (IA-32) with the 80386 in 1985 and to 64-bit (x86-64) by AMD in 2003. Today x86 dominates desktop, server, and many embedded applications, though its power consumption is a concern for spacecraft.

x86 / Intel & AMD

The dominant CISC architecture in computing. Developed by Intel in 1978 and later adopted by AMD. Powers the majority of desktop, server, and some embedded systems.

ProsCons
Massive software ecosystem — compatible with Windows, Linux, macOS, countless appsHigh power consumption and heat generation
Highest single-thread performance of any architectureComplex instruction decode and pipeline — more silicon area
Rich instruction set — single instructions can replace multiple RISC instructionsBackwards compatibility constraints limit architectural innovation
Elite process technology — Intel/AMD lead in node manufacturingLicense restricted — only Intel and AMD can produce x86
RISC Architecture (Reduced Instruction Set Computer)

Capable of simple instruction sets, faster execution, and less power. Known for high performance, energy efficiency, and ability to handle multiple tasks simultaneously.

The RISC philosophy originated at UC Berkeley in 1980 under David Patterson's RISC-I project. ARM (Acorn RISC Machine) began as a collaboration between Acorn Computers and VLSI Technology in 1985 — the ARM1 had just 25,000 transistors (roughly the same as the 8086) but consumed only 0.1 W. Today ARM powers billions of mobile and embedded devices. RISC-V, an open-standard ISA, started at UC Berkeley in 2010 and is gaining traction in space applications for its openness and customizability.

ARM (Advanced RISC Machine)

The dominant RISC architecture in mobile, embedded, and increasingly server/space applications. ARM Holdings designs the architecture and licenses it to manufacturers who implement their own chips.

ProsCons
Very low power consumption — ideal for battery-powered and spacecraft systemsRoyalty licensing costs (though lower than proprietary ISAs)
Ecosystem is massive — extensive toolchains, RTOS support, software librariesArchitecture fragmentation (ARMv7, ARMv8, ARMv9, Thumb, AArch64)
Licensing model allows customization for specific mission needsNot fully open — licensees must pay for access
Radiation-hardened variants available (BAE RAD750, Cobham ARM)Performance per clock is lower than x86 for complex workloads

PowerPC

A RISC architecture originally developed by the AIM alliance (Apple, IBM, Motorola) in 1991. Known for high reliability, used extensively in aerospace, automotive, and networking.

ProsCons
Excellent radiation tolerance — many rad-hard variants existDeclining commercial ecosystem — fewer consumer products
High reliability — proven in decades of space missionsHigher power consumption than ARM at equivalent performance
Strong floating-point performance for scientific computingLimited software ecosystem compared to ARM or x86
Deterministic execution — ideal for hard real-time systemsArchitecture is legacy, no major new designs

TRICORE

A 32-bit RISC architecture developed by Siemens/Infineon for real-time embedded systems, particularly automotive and industrial control. Features a unique tri-core design optimized for deterministic task execution.

ProsCons
Extremely deterministic — predictable execution for hard real-timePrimarily single-source (Infineon)

Superior interrupt handling with minimal latencyNot designed for general-purpose computing
Automotive-grade reliability (AEC-Q100 qualified)Higher cost per unit than ARM alternatives

RH850

A 32-bit RISC architecture from Renesas designed for automotive body control, chassis, and safety systems. Succeeded the earlier V850 architecture with higher performance and expanded safety features.

GTM IP (Generic Timer Module)

While technically a timer/co-processor IP core rather than a standalone CPU, GTM IP is a RISC-like programmable microcontroller used in automotive and space systems for precise timing, PWM generation, and motor control.

MIPS (Microprocessor without Interlocked Pipeline Stages)

A pioneering RISC architecture designed by John L. Hennessy at Stanford in 1981. Was widely used in routers, game consoles, and embedded systems but has largely been displaced by ARM.

ProsCons
Clean, simple ISA — easy to implement and teachDeclining ecosystem — most vendors have moved to ARM
Historically strong in networking and telecomLimited modern software and toolchain support
Very efficient pipeline — good performance per wattNo major new designs since 2010s

COLDFIRE

A 32-bit RISC architecture from Freescale (now NXP), derived from the Motorola 68000 family. Widely used in industrial control, avionics, and legacy embedded systems.

ProsCons
Code-compatible with legacy 68000 family — easy migration pathLegacy architecture — limited new development
Proven in safety-critical industrial and avionics systemsLower performance than modern ARM alternatives
Low interrupt latency — good for real-time controlSmall and shrinking ecosystem
Extensive peripheral integration (CAN, Ethernet, USB)Higher power consumption than equivalent ARM MCUs

RISC-V

An open-standard RISC ISA originally developed at UC Berkeley in 2010. Unlike all other RISC architectures, RISC-V is completely free and open — anyone can implement a processor without licensing fees. Rapidly gaining adoption in space, AI, and embedded systems.

ProsCons
Completely open and royalty-free — no licensing costsEcosystem is still maturing — fewer tools, RTOS ports

Customizable — ideal for FPGA-based soft processors in spacecraftLess software and library support than ARM

Strong academic and government backingHardware maturity still behind ARM for high-end designs

1.2 Memory

A device that temporarily holds data and instructions for immediate access. Memory is broadly categorized into Volatile Memory (VM) and Non-Volatile Memory (NVM), which differ in how they retain data when power is removed.

CategoryRetentionSpeedUse Case
Volatile (VM)Loses data when power is offFastActive processing, temporary storage
Non-Volatile (NVM)Retains data without powerSlowerLong-term storage, firmware, config

In spacecraft, ECC (Error Correcting Code) memory is critical — ionizing radiation can flip individual bits (single-event upsets / SEUs). Many rad-hard memory devices include built-in ECC or utilize scrubbing techniques where memory is periodically read, corrected, and rewritten to prevent error accumulation.

Volatile Memory (VM)

A type of computer memory that temporarily stores data and loses that data when power is removed. Called volatile because the stored information is not persistent and does not survive power cycles.

Key Characteristics

  • Data retention — loses all stored data when power is disconnected or turned off. Unsuitable for long-term storage.
  • Speed — fast read and write operations compared to NVM. Used for quick access and manipulation of data during operations.
  • Usage — primarily for quick data storage, running apps, storing actively-processed data, managed by the OS.

DRAM & SRAM

The two main types of volatile RAM — DRAM is dense and cheap but needs constant refresh; SRAM is fast and static but expensive.

DRAM was invented by Robert Dennard at IBM in 1966. SRAM predates DRAM and was used in early core memory systems. Both remain essential: DRAM provides affordable main memory, while SRAM enables the cache hierarchy that keeps modern CPUs fed with data. For spacecraft, DRAM's data remanence at low temperatures means sensitive data can persist longer than expected after power-off — a security concern.

Key Differences Between Generations

  • Voltage — each generation drops voltage significantly (2.5V → ~1.0V), reducing power consumption
  • Prefetch buffer — doubled from 2n to 16n, allowing more data per memory access
  • Banks — increased from 4 to 32+, enabling more parallel accesses
  • Bandwidth — DDR1 peaks at 3.2 GB/s (PC-3200), DDR5 exceeds 60+ GB/s
  • DDR5 introduced on-die ECC, dual 32-bit sub-channels per DIMM, and an on-module PMIC (power management IC)
  • DDR6 (in development) is expected to use PAM-4 signaling (4-level pulse amplitude modulation) to achieve even higher data rates without doubling pin speed

SDRAM (Synchronous DRAM) was first standardized by JEDEC in 1993. DDR SDRAM doubled the transfer rate without increasing the clock by transferring data on both edges of the clock signal. Each generation roughly doubles bandwidth while cutting voltage — DDR1 modules peaked at 3.2 GB/s using 2.5V, while DDR5 modules deliver 60+ GB/s at just 1.1V. For spacecraft, DDR memory is often derated (run below max speed) and paired with ECC to mitigate radiation-induced single-event upsets (SEUs).

Non-Volatile Memory (NVM)

A type of computer memory that permanently stores data and retains data when power is removed. Called non-volatile because the stored information is persistent and survives power cycles.

Key Characteristics

  • Data retention — retains stored data across power cycles, maintains data integrity without power
  • Speed — typically slower read and write operations, but offers data persistence
  • Usage — long-term data storage, firmware storage

SPI NOR is a type of flash memory that uses the SPI communication protocol to interface with hardware.

Intel introduced NOR flash in 1988 as a high-reliability, random-access memory technology. Toshiba's Fujio Masuoka invented NAND flash the following year (1989), prioritizing density and cost over random access. Both were breakthroughs over EEPROM — Fujio Masuoka also invented the original floating-gate EEPROM technology that made flash possible.

1.3 FPGA (Field-Programmable Gate Array)

A type of configurable integrated circuit that can be repeatedly programmed after manufacturing. FPGAs consist of a grid-connected array of programmable logic blocks that can be configured to implement custom digital circuits.

  • Reconfigurable after deployment
  • Used for parallel processing, custom I/O, and hardware acceleration
  • Common in spacecraft for sensor processing, communication interfaces, and glue logic
  • Radiation-tolerant variants available for space applications

The first FPGA was invented by Ross Freeman at Xilinx in 1985. The XC2064 had just 64 logic blocks and 58 I/O pins using 2 µm technology — a far cry from modern FPGAs with millions of logic cells. FPGAs are widely used in spacecraft because they can be reconfigured after launch, allowing bug fixes and feature upgrades without a hardware respin. NASA's James Webb Space Telescope uses radiation-tolerant FPGAs for instrument control and data processing.

1.4 Storage — SSD (NVMe vs SATA)

  • Two different communication protocols used to connect SSDs
  • SATA — caps at ~600 MB/s for read/write/erase. Higher latency.
  • NVMe — connects via motherboard PCIe lanes, can do 3,500 – 14,000+ MB/s. Lower latency, parallel command queues.
  • SATA is budget-friendly; NVMe is best all-around.

Flash-based SSDs began replacing hard drives in the late 2000s. SATA was originally designed for spinning disks, which is why it caps at ~600 MB/s with high latency and a single command queue. NVMe, released in 2011, was built from the ground up for flash — it connects directly via PCIe lanes and supports up to 65,535 parallel command queues. In spacecraft, radiation-hardened SSDs with custom controllers are used for bulk data storage, often in RAID-1 configurations for redundancy.

1.5 Error Protection — EDAC & RAID

EDAC (Error Detection and Correction)

A system built into hardware and software designed to identify and repair corrupted data in NVM or VM.

RAID-0 & RAID-1

  • RAID-1 — mirrored storage using 2+ NVMe SSDs. System can still run if one drive fails.
  • RAID-0 — stripes data for speed (read/write) but offers no fault tolerance.

2. Flight Software (FSW)

Flight Software (FSW) is the intelligence of the CDH subsystem — the operating system, applications, drivers, and bootloader that control every aspect of the mission. FSW runs on the OBC hardware, schedules tasks, manages resources, and provides a stable environment for mission-critical applications. In space, a Real-Time OS (RTOS) is the standard choice because missing a deadline can mean losing the spacecraft.

OS at a Glance

Hard Real-Time OS (Hard RTOS)
  • Deterministic — worst-case execution time (WCET) is provably bounded
  • Missing a deadline is treated as a system failure (may cause loss of spacecraft)
  • Avoids virtual memory, dynamic allocation, and caching surprises
  • Typical response: 1–100 µs interrupt latency

ARINC 653 is the standard for space-grade partitioning. It defines fixed time windows and memory regions that guarantee one partition cannot interfere with another — critical when safety software and payload software run on the same CPU.

Soft Real-Time OS (Soft RTOS)
  • Timing is important but occasional missed deadlines are tolerable
  • Uses full MMU and virtual memory for process isolation
  • Common in multimedia streaming, telemetry packing, file serving
  • Typical response: 1–10 ms average latency
General-Purpose OS (GPOS)
  • Optimized for average-case throughput, fairness, and user interaction
  • No real-time guarantees — scheduling is fair-share (CFS, round-robin)
  • Rich ecosystem: file systems, networking, drivers, package management
  • Used on ground segment, crew laptops, and non-critical payloads
  • Can be made "harder" with RT_PREEMPT patches (Linux) or INtime (Windows)

2.2 Radiation Effects — LET & TID

  • Linear Energy Transfer (LET) — the amount of energy an ionizing particle transfers to material traversed per unit distance. Describes the action of radiation into matter (e.g., proton, heavy ion). Measured in MeV (megaelectron-volts).
  • Total Ionizing Dose (TID) — cumulative buildup of energy deposited in a material by ionizing radiation over time. A charge buildup resulting from exposure to ionizing radiation. Measured in krad (1000x rad).

Time Distribution — RTC / PPS / PPM

Spacecraft timekeeping is critical for mission operations. Every data point needs a timestamp, events must be sequenced accurately, and orbit maneuvers depend on precise timing. The onboard clock is built around three concepts:

  • Oscillator — a precision crystal (TCXO, OCXO) that generates a stable frequency (e.g. 10 MHz). The oscillator's PPM (Parts Per Million) rating states its drift: ±1 ppm = ±1 µs per second = ±86 ms per day.
  • PPS (Pulse Per Second) — a hardware signal generated by dividing the oscillator frequency down to 1 Hz. Each rising edge marks the start of a new UTC/TAI second. FSW latches the PPS interrupt to update the onboard time counter.
  • Time at Tone (TaT) — the absolute time (date + time) snapshot associated with a specific PPS edge. The ground station sends TaT updates to correct accumulated drift.

Why Timing Matters

  • Data time-tagging — science observations, telemetry, and event logs all need accurate timestamps to correlate across instruments and with ground-based measurements
  • Orbit & attitude control — thruster firings, wheel desaturations, and pointing maneuvers depend on knowing when each action occurs; a 10 ms error in a burn start can miss the target orbit
  • Subsystem coordination — the CDH, power, thermal, and payload subsystems need a common time reference to synchronize data-taking, mode changes, and fault responses
  • Ground contact windows — the spacecraft must know when it will be in view of a ground station to schedule passes and point the antenna

How to Account for Timing Errors

Practical Guidelines

  • Oscillator selection — TCXO (±0.5–5 ppm) is typical for LEO missions with frequent ground contact. OCXO (±0.001–0.1 ppm) for deep-space where passes are days apart. For sub-ppm stability, ovenized space-grade oscillators (e.g. from Microchip, Q-Tech, Rakon) are used.
  • Temperature effects — crystal oscillators drift with temperature. A TCXO compensates electronically; an OCXO heats the crystal to a constant temperature. In LEO, thermal cycling (±100 °C every 90 min) makes TCXO/OCXO essential.
  • Aging — crystals age, shifting frequency by 0.5–5 ppm over 10 years. The drift is predictable and can be pre-compensated with a calibration table.
  • Time format — most space missions use TAI (atomic time) onboard and convert to UTC for ground distribution. Leap seconds are handled in FSW, never in hardware.

How Bus Choice Affects Harness & Connectors

The physical layer drives mass and cost more than any protocol difference:

  • MIL-STD-1553 — each bus requires a single twisted pair with isolation transformers at each stub. The harness is light but the transformers add mass. Can only have ~30–40 remote terminals per bus.
  • SpaceWire — each link requires 2 differential pairs + a strobe line (9-pin micro-D connector). For a 4-port switch, expect 36 pins just for data. The cable bundles become heavy on large spacecraft.
  • CAN — only 2 wires (CAN_H, CAN_L) with no isolation transformers. The lightest harness option. Supports up to ~120 nodes per bus. CAN-FD maintains the same 2-wire interface.
  • I²C — only 2 wires but requires pull-up resistors. The capacitive load of long cables or many nodes degrades speed. Not recommended for cables longer than ~1 m in space.
  • Hybrid architectures — most spacecraft use a combination: MIL-STD-1553 or CAN for command/response, SpaceWire for payload data, and I²C/SPI for chip-level interconnects. This minimizes harness mass while meeting all data-rate needs.

CAN Variants & Other Bus Technologies

  • CAN 2.0A — standard 11-bit identifier (up to 2032 messages on a bus). 8-byte payload per frame.
  • CAN 2.0B — extended 29-bit identifier (~536M messages). 8-byte payload. Backward-compatible with 2.0A.
  • CAN-FD (Flexible Data-Rate) — doubles the data phase bitrate (up to 8 Mb/s) and supports up to 64 bytes per frame. CRC enhanced for improved error detection. Becoming the standard for new spacecraft designs.
  • CAN XL — in development (CiA 610), targeting up to 20 Mb/s and 2048-byte payloads. Not yet widely used in space.
  • SpaceFibre — the successor to SpaceWire. Runs over a single differential pair at up to 6.25 Gb/s per lane. Supports galvanic isolation, quality-of-service (QoS), and FDIR built-in. Emerging in next-gen very-high-rate missions.
  • TTEthernet (Time-Triggered Ethernet) — standard Ethernet hardware with deterministic, time-triggered traffic classes. Used in avionics (Airbus A350) and being evaluated for spacecraft. Supports mixed criticality on one cable.

SpaceWire was developed by ESA in the 1990s and is now widely used on NASA and ESA missions for high-rate data. MIL-STD-1553 dates from the 1970s and remains the standard for military and aerospace command/response buses due to its deterministic behavior and galvanic isolation. CAN, originally developed by Bosch for automotive, is increasingly used in CubeSats for its simplicity and multi-master capability. The trend in next-generation spacecraft (e.g. ESA's OPS-SAT, NASA's HALO) is toward CAN-FD for command/response and SpaceFibre for high-rate payload links.

Command & Telemetry — Framing, Standards & Packetization

The CDH subsystem processes commands uplinked from the ground and packages telemetry for downlink. FSW handles the protocol stack — from raw RF frames up to application-level commands and telemetry packets. Multiple standards coexist depending on the mission type, agency, and data rate.

How Framing Works

Data is not sent as a raw stream — it is wrapped in frames that add synchronization markers, addressing, error detection, and sometimes encryption. The frame is the smallest unit of transmission on the space link. Multiple frames make up a pass.

  • Frame size — typically 64–4096 bytes, set at mission design time. Larger frames reduce overhead ratio but increase the bit-error penalty (a single corrupted bit can drop the whole frame). Most LEO missions use 1024–2048 byte frames as a balance.
  • Packet vs frame — a frame is the physical/link layer container; a packet is the data unit above it. One frame can hold one large packet or multiple small packets (packet multiplexing).
  • Fill data — if the last packet in a frame does not fill the data field, FSW adds fill bytes (typically all-zeros or 0xAA). The receiver discards fill based on the packet length field. Fill is wasted bandwidth but keeps framing aligned.
  • Oversized data (segmentation) — if a packet exceeds the frame data field size, FSW segments it across multiple frames. Each segment carries a sequence counter so the ground can reassemble. The CCSDS Packet Version 2 format includes a "first/last" marker for this purpose.

Framing & Packetization Details

  • Frame overhead — the sync, header, CRC, and tail of a CCSDS frame add ~18–24 bytes. If your frame is 200 bytes and you send one 20-byte packet, you waste 160 bytes (80% overhead). FSW achieves efficiency by packing multiple packets.
  • Packet multiplexing — multiple small packets (housekeeping, event messages, science samples) are concatenated in the data field. The receiver uses the length field in each packet header to extract them.
  • Buffer model — FSW allocates a transmit buffer the size of one frame. Packets are written into it sequentially. When the buffer is full (or a timer fires), the frame is sent. This creates a trade-off: larger buffers = better packing efficiency but more memory and longer latency.
  • Segmenting large data — a 10 MB science image cannot fit in one frame. FSW divides it into segments, each with a unique sequence number and a "not-last" flag in the packet header. The ground reassembles by collecting all segments and checking for gaps.
  • Fill packets — if the remaining space in a frame buffer is too small for the next packet, FSW inserts a fill packet (CCSDS fill type = 0x0000) to pad to the frame boundary. The receiver identifies fill packets by their ID and discards them.
  • Virtual Channels (VCs) — CCSDS frames can carry a VC identifier in the header, allowing multiple logical data streams (e.g., VC0 for real-time telemetry, VC1 for stored science, VC2 for commands) to share one physical link. Each VC gets its own sequence count for reliable reassembly.
  • Store-and-Forward — when the spacecraft is out of contact, telemetry is stored on the mass-memory SSD in the same framed format. During the next pass, FSW reads the stored frames and transmits them — no reformatting needed.

CCSDS was formed in 1982 by the world's space agencies to create common data handling standards. Before CCSDS, every mission used bespoke frame formats, making cross-support between agencies nearly impossible. The most recent major addition is CCSDS 766.1 (CFDP), ratified in 2022, which enables reliable file transfer with automatic retransmission — critical for deep-space missions where round-trip delays are measured in minutes to hours.

FDIR — Fault Detection, Isolation & Recovery

FDIR is the safety net of the CDH subsystem. It continuously monitors spacecraft health and takes autonomous action when something goes wrong — from resetting a crashed task to switching to a redundant unit or entering safe mode. In deep-space missions where light-speed delay can be minutes to hours, FDIR must work without ground intervention.

FDIR Hierarchy (3 Levels)

LevelScopeResponse TimeExample Actions
L1 — ComponentSingle device, task, or sensorµs – msReset a crashed task, power-cycle an instrument, mask a failed sensor
L2 — Subsystem / UnitRedundant unit or subsystemms – sSwitch to cold-redundant OBC, failover to redundant IMU, reconfigure data bus
L3 — Spacecraft (Safe Mode)Entire spacecrafts – minHalt non-critical loads, point solar arrays at sun, wait for ground commands

Fault Detection Mechanisms

  • Watchdog Timers — a hardware timer that must be serviced (kicked) by FSW at a regular interval. If FSW hangs or crashes, the watchdog expires and resets the OBC. Best practice: use a dual watchdog — one in the CPU (monitored by RTOS idle task) and one external (monitored by a small microcontroller or a CPLD).
  • Health Monitors — voltage, current, and temperature sensors with configurable high/low limits. Exceeding a limit triggers an interrupt that FDIR handles immediately. Example: if the 5V rail drops to 4.5V, the health monitor alerts FSW to switch to a redundant power converter before the OBC browns out.
  • Heartbeat Messages — each subsystem periodically sends a "I'm alive" message on the data bus. The CDH expects these messages at fixed intervals. A missing heartbeat triggers an L1 or L2 action depending on the subsystem's criticality.
  • Data Bus Monitoring — bus controllers (e.g., MIL-STD-1553 bus controller, CAN controller) detect bus-off conditions, CRC errors, and missing acknowledgments. These are reported to the FDIR service as health events.
  • Memory Scrubbing — for ECC-protected memory, the EDAC hardware corrects single-bit errors automatically. FDIR only needs to act if multi-bit errors (uncorrectable) are detected — typically a memory reset or OBC failover.

Fault Isolation Strategies

  • Galvanic Isolation — data buses (MIL-STD-1553, SpaceWire) use transformers or optocouplers to prevent a fault on one node from propagating to the bus. This is a hardware-level isolation that FDIR relies on.
  • Power Switching — each subsystem is on an individually switchable power channel. FDIR can power-cycle a malfunctioning unit without affecting others. Latching current limiters (LCLs) prevent overcurrent from dragging down the bus.
  • Redundancy Domains — critical units have hot (powered) or cold (unpowered) spares. FDIR manages the switchover: for hot spares, the transition is seamless; for cold spares, there is a power-on and initialization delay that must be accounted for in the FDIR timeline.
  • Fail-Silent / Fail-Stop — components are designed to fail silent: if a device detects an internal fault, it ceases bus communication so it does not corrupt the bus. TMR voters mask the failure at the system level.

Recovery Patterns

  • Retry — for transient faults (e.g., a CRC error on a command), FSW retries the operation up to N times before escalating.
  • Reset & Restore — for software faults, the watchdog triggers a CPU reset. The bootloader checks a reset counter: if the count exceeds a threshold (e.g., 3 resets in 10 minutes), the spacecraft enters safe mode to avoid a reset loop.
  • Failover — for hardware faults, the OBC switches to a redundant unit. State (time, mode, configuration) must be preserved or quickly rebuilt on the spare. Cold-spare failover can take seconds — during this time the spacecraft is blind.
  • Degraded Mode — if a sensor fails, FSW uses a model-based estimate instead. If an actuator fails, FSW reconfigures the control algorithm. The mission continues at reduced capability until ground can diagnose.
  • Safe Mode Entry & Exit — the spacecraft enters safe mode autonomously (e.g., 3 watchdog resets, critical bus undervoltage, loss of attitude control). It orients to a power-safe attitude, disables non-critical loads, and waits for ground. Ground then diagnoses the root cause, uploads a recovery plan, and commands a return to normal mode.

FDIR Design Principles

  • Single-fault tolerant — the system must survive any single point of failure (one component fails → no mission impact). Dual or triple redundancy for critical functions.
  • No single point of failure in FDIR itself — the watchdog that checks FSW must be independent of the CPU. The health monitor that checks the power supply must have its own reference.
  • Debounce faults — transient faults (e.g., a cosmic ray SEU causing a false temperature reading) should not trigger a major recovery action. Use a persistence counter: a fault must be present for N consecutive samples before acting.
  • Ground override — FDIR actions can be inhibited or reconfigured by ground command. This is essential during critical operations (e.g., orbit insertion) where the spacecraft must not autonomously safe-mode.
  • Logging — every FDIR event is logged with a timestamp, the fault signature, and the action taken. This log is the primary tool for post-event analysis by the ground team.

A well-designed FDIR strategy is critical for deep-space missions where light-speed delay makes real-time ground intervention impossible. NASA's Mars rovers and ESA's comet-chasing Rosetta both carried sophisticated onboard FDIR that kept the spacecraft alive through anomalies without ground contact. Rosetta famously survived a 957-day deep-space hibernation and autonomously woke up, oriented to Earth, and established communication — a pure FDIR achievement. On the near-Earth side, SpaceX's Dragon capsule performs continuous FDIR during crewed flight, with redundant flight computers, cross-strapped sensors, and autonomous abort capability.

Designing a CDH subsystem begins with the mission's processing, memory, and data-handling requirements. The OBC hardware must run the flight software within real-time deadlines, store enough telemetry between ground contacts, and communicate reliably with every other subsystem — all while surviving the radiation environment.

The typical design workflow:

  1. Define processing requirements — control loop rates, data throughput, FSW complexity, radiation tolerance
  2. Select the processor architecture — ARM, RISC-V, PowerPC, or x86; single-core vs. multi-core; FPGA companion or SoC
  3. Size the memory hierarchy — RAM for code + data, NOR Flash for XIP code storage, NAND/SSD for bulk telemetry
  4. Choose the data bus topology — CAN vs. MIL-STD-1553 vs. SpaceWire; harness mass, speed, and redundancy trades
  5. Design the FDIR strategy — watchdog hierarchy, health monitorsost, and radiation constraints tighten the design

What Is Derating?

Derating is operating a component below its rated maximum to improve reliability and reduce failure rate in the space environment. For the CDH subsystem, derating applies across every component:

ComponentDerating Example
ProcessorRun at ≤80% of max rated clock frequency; keep core voltage within 90% of nominal
FPGAUse ≤50% of available logic cells; keep I/O toggle rate ≤70% of max
MemoryLimit memory access rate to ≤70% of datasheet maximum; derate operating temperature by 20 °C below abs max
Bus transceiversDrive bus at ≤80% of rated cable length; keep signal swing below ±90% of max differential voltage
OscillatorOperate within ±50% of specified temperature range margin; derate vibration tolerance by 20%
Power supply (OBC-side)Limit continuous load to ≤70% of rated output; derate input voltage range by 10% on each end
ConnectorsUse ≤60% of rated current per pin; derate temperature rating by 20 °C

Derating margins are specified by standards. ECSS-Q-ST-30-11C (European) and MIL-STD-975 (US) define derating factors per component type and mission class. Higher-class missions (NASA Class A, ESA Class 1) require more conservative derating.

Typical Design Order

CDH design is a dependency chain — each decision constrains the next. A practical order:

  1. Processing architecture — CPU type first, because it determines software ecosystem, rad-hardness path, and companion components
  2. Memory architecture — RAM + NVM sizes and types, driven by FSW footprint and data volume
  3. Data bus topology — what talks to what, at what speed, with what redundancy
  4. Timekeeping — oscillator grade and PPS distribution once you know the subsystems that need sync
  5. Redundancy & FDIR — once you know the criticality of each function, decide what to duplicate and how to recover
  6. Physical integration — card form factor, connectors, power dissipation, mass — constrained by everything above
  7. Software architecture — RTOS, bootloader, image update — runs on the hardware decided above

In practice, this is iterative. A chosen CPU may not fit the power budget, forcing a step back. The data bus choice may limit which processors are viable. Expect 2–3 passes through the full chain to converge.

Worked Example: 6U Cubesat OBC Selection

Step 1: Mission Requirements

Scenario: 6U Earth observation cubesat at 500 km SSO. The CDH subsystem must handle:

FunctionRequirement
Attitude control loop100 Hz control rate, 1 kHz sensor read rate (gyro, star tracker, magnetometer)
Payload data handlingCamera produces 12 Mpixel images at 10-bit depth, 1 image per orbit. Process and compress: ~12 MB per orbit
Telemetry storageStore 72 hours of continuous housekeeping (100 parameters at 1 Hz): ~2.5 GB
Command processingAccept and validate uplink commands (up to 1 kB per command), distribute to subsystems
Radiation environmentTID ~15 krad over 3 years (500 km SSO, 2.5 mm Al shielding)

The key driver is the 100 Hz control loop with deterministic timing — an RTOS is needed, and the processor must have enough throughput for control + data compression + telemetry packing simultaneously.

Step 4: Bus Architecture

The CDH data bus must connect all subsystems. For a 6U cubesat, a single CAN 2.0B bus at 500 kbps covers all command/response and housekeeping traffic. The payload camera connects via a dedicated SPI link (20 Mbps) for high-speed image transfer to the OBC's external memory.

BusConnected NodesDataSpeed
CAN 2.0BOBC, EPS, COMMS, ADCS, Thermal ControllerCommands, telemetry, mode changes, fault reports500 kbps
SPI (dedicated)OBC → Payload Camera (NVM)Raw image data → external memory20 Mbps
UARTOBC → GPS receiverNMEA position/velocity sentences115,200 bps

This simple bus architecture keeps harness mass low (~50 g), connector count minimal (1 micro-D for CAN + power), and fault isolation straightforward. CAN's built-in priority arbitration ensures command messages always get through even under bus load.

Step 5: Convergence

The preliminary CDH design:

  • Processor: STM32H743 (ARM Cortex-M7, 480 MHz, 1 MB SRAM)
  • Code storage: 128 Mb Quad-SPI NOR Flash (XIP for fast boot)
  • Data storage: 8 MB external PSRAM (buffer) + 1 GB SLC microSD (telemetry/images)
  • RTOS: FreeRTOS with preemptive scheduling, tickless idle for power saving
  • Data bus: CAN 2.0B at 500 kbps + dedicated SPI for payload + UART for GPS
  • FDIR: External watchdog + software TMR on critical state + ECC on external memory
  • Total OBC power: ~0.6 W (continuous) to ~1.5 W (payload processing peak)
  • Total OBC mass: ~150 g (single PC/104-sized board)

Now iterate:

  • Could FreeRTOS meet the 100 Hz control deadline? The STM32H7 at 480 MHz can execute ~50,000 instructions per control cycle at 100 Hz — well within margin for a simple PD controller. If the control law becomes more complex (e.g., LQR with Kalman filter), consider moving to the PolarFire SoC for dedicated FPGA acceleration.
  • Is 1 GB of data storage sufficient? With 12 MB per image + 2.5 GB for 72-hour telemetry: if downlink is 1 pass per day with 10 MB data volume, the buffer fills at ~37 MB/day. A 1 GB card holds > 27 days of data — adequate margin.
  • Could CAN-FD replace CAN 2.0B? CAN-FD (8 Mbps data phase) would allow firmware updates over the bus 16× faster, useful if the COMMS link is the bottleneck. Consider upgrading if over-the-air updates are needed.
  • Can the radiation tolerance be improved without changing the processor? Shielding the OBC with 1 mm additional Al adds ~30 g but reduces TID from ~15 krad to ~8 krad, well within COTS margins. A cost-effective mitigation.

Each iteration tightens the design toward a CDH subsystem that meets the mission's processing, storage, and data-handling needs within the mass, power, and cost budgets of a 6U cubesat.
