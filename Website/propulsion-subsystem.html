Propulsion Subsystem

The Propulsion subsystem provides the thrust needed for orbit insertion, station-keeping, attitude control (through thrusters), and end-of-life disposal. Propulsion is one of the highest-risk subsystems — a failure can mean losing the mission — and one of the most tightly coupled to the spacecraft mass budget, as propellant is often a significant fraction of total wet mass.

Before You Dive In — The Big Picture

The Propulsion subsystem provides the delta-v (Δv) that changes the spacecraft's velocity — for orbit insertion, station-keeping, momentum management, and disposal. It is unique among subsystems because it consumes its own mass (propellant) to produce thrust, making every kilogram of propellant a direct trade against payload mass.

Every propulsion subsystem is defined by five core elements:

ElementWhat it does
PropellantThe reaction mass expelled to produce thrust. Type determines Isp, storage temperature, tank material compatibility, and handling safety.
TankStores the propellant under pressure. Material, shape, and diaphragm/bladder type depend on propellant and mission duration.
Feed SystemPipes, valves, regulators, and filters that deliver propellant from tank to thruster at the right pressure and flow rate.
ThrusterThe device that expands and expels the propellant to produce thrust. Nozzle design, thrust level, and specific impulse vary by type.
PressurizationThe system (regulator, pressurant tank, or blowdown) that maintains propellant tank pressure as propellant is consumed.

These elements are tightly coupled: propellant type drives tank material and pressurization method, feed system design determines minimum impulse bit, and thruster placement defines the spacecraft torque and delta-v capability in each axis.

1. Propulsion Fundamentals

Specific impulse, thrust, total impulse, and delta-v are the fundamental parameters that define any propulsion system. These fundamentals connect the propulsion subsystem to the broader mission design.

Key Parameters
ParameterSymbolDefinition
Specific impulseIsp (seconds)Thrust per unit propellant mass flow rate; higher Isp = more efficient propellant use
ThrustF (N)Force produced by the thruster; determines acceleration and maneuver duration
Total impulseItot (N·s)Integral of thrust over total burn time; product of Isp × propellant mass × g₀
Delta-vΔv (m/s)Change in velocity the propulsion system can deliver; calculated from Tsiolkovsky rocket equation
Minimum impulse bitMin Ibit (N·s)Smallest impulse the thruster can deliver; drives fine attitude control capability
The Rocket Equation

Δv = Isp × g₀ × ln(m₀ / mf)

Where m₀ is initial mass (wet), mf is final mass (dry), and g₀ = 9.80665 m/s². The equation shows that achieving higher Δv requires either higher Isp (better propellant efficiency) or more propellant mass fraction. A propellant mass fraction of 50% gives Δv ≈ 0.69 × Isp × g₀.

3. System Architecture

The propulsion system architecture defines how propellant flows from tank to thruster, how pressure is managed, and how redundancy and isolation are implemented.

Feed System Types
TypeDescriptionWhen Used
BlowdownPressurant gas in same tank as propellant; pressure drops as propellant is consumedSimple systems, small spacecraft, low total impulse
RegulatedSeparate pressurant tank + regulator maintains constant feed pressureHigh total impulse, precision maneuvers, bipropellant systems
Pressure-fedHigh-pressure pressurant forces propellant to thruster; no pumpMost spacecraft (simpler than turbopump)
Pump-fed (turbopump / electric)Pump boosts propellant pressure; used in launch vehicles and some large spacecraftHigh-thrust, high-pressure applications
Tank Types
TypeConfigurationProsCons
DiaphragmElastomeric diaphragm separates propellant from pressurantPositive propellant expulsion; reliable; zero-g compatibleLimited cycle life; material compatibility limits
BladderFlexible bladder inside tank (propellant inside bladder)Positive expulsion; well-proven heritageBladder material limits temperature and propellant
Surface tension (PMD)Propellant management device uses capillary forcesNo moving parts; long life; zero-g compatibleMore complex design; higher cost
Piston / positive displacementMechanical piston pushes propellant outVery reliable expulsion; simpleAdds moving part; heavier
Architecture Diagram (Typical)

A typical monopropellant blowdown system: Pressurant (He) → Propellant tank (with diaphragm) → Filter → Latching valve → Thruster. A regulated system adds: High-pressure pressurant tank → Regulator → Propellant tank → Filter → Latching valves → Thruster bank. Electric propulsion adds: Power processing unit (PPU) → Flow controller → Thruster. Redundant valves and isolation pyros are included for fault tolerance.

4. Mission Planning

Mission planning determines how much propellant is needed, how burns are scheduled, and how the system is managed over the mission lifetime to ensure sufficient delta-v remains for disposal.

Disposal burnFinal burn to deorbit (raise perigee into atmosphere) or move to graveyard orbit
Propellant Budgeting

Propellant mass is calculated from the delta-v budget using the rocket equation. Key considerations: pressurant mass (for blowdown or regulated systems must be included), residuals (unusable propellant left in tank due to slosh, PMD limits, or pressurant blowdown), and boil-off (for cryogenic propellants). Typical usable propellant fraction is 90–98% of loaded mass.

5. Integration & Testing

Propulsion is one of the most hazardous subsystems to integrate and test. Propellant loading, leak checking, and safe handling procedures are critical program activities.

Integration Activities
ActivityDescription
Component-level acceptanceProof pressure, burst pressure, leak testing of tank; functional test of valves, regulators, transducers
System-level leak checkHelium mass spectrometer leak test of all welded joints and fittings; typically < 1×10⁻⁶ scc/s He
Propellant loadingPerformed at launch site; hydrazine loading requires full hazmat PPE; green propellants reduce safety burden
PressurizationPressurant tank filled to operating pressure; regulator set points verified
End-to-end functional testValve cycling, thruster fire test (if permitted), heater checkout, temperature sensor verification
Testing Sequence
TestPurpose
Proof pressure testVerify tank and tubing can withstand 1.5× operating pressure without permanent deformation
Burst pressure test (qualification only)Destructive test on qualification unit to verify 4× safety factor
Leak test (external and internal)Verify all seals hold; internal leak test checks valve seat leakage
Flow testMeasure pressure drop at expected flow rates; verify feed system delivers required mass flow
Vibration testVerify structural integrity under launch vibration; propellant is simulated with water or non-toxic simulant
Safety & Handling
  • Hydrazine is toxic, carcinogenic, and flammable — requires hazmat suits, double-walled piping, and scrubbers.
  • Green propellants (LMP-103S, AF-M315E) are less toxic but may still require special handling due to high temperature exhaust.
  • High-pressure gas operations follow strict procedures: pressure relief devices, burst disks, remote fill/vent.
  • Propulsion integration is typically the last major activity before spacecraft mate to launcher — delays here impact the entire launch campaign.
  • End-of-life passivation: venting remaining propellant and pressurant to prevent explosions during disposal.

6. Propulsion Design Walkthrough

This walkthrough goes from mission requirements to a sized propulsion system for a 6U cubesat. The same process applies to any spacecraft — only the numbers change.

Step 1: Mission Requirements & Δv Budget

Scenario: A 6U Earth observation cubesat at 400 km LEO, dry mass 8 kg. The mission requires controlled deorbit at end of life and station-keeping for 3 years.

ManeuverΔv (m/s)Notes
Drag makeup (3 years)~30At 400 km, atmospheric drag is moderate; ~10 m/s/year
Collision avoidance (3 × 2 m/s)6Typical smallsat allocation
End-of-life deorbit~80Lower perigee to < 200 km for < 25-year decay
Reserve / margin (20%)~24Contingency for anomalies and extended mission
Total Δv~140 m/s

The key trade: cold gas (low Isp ~60 s, very simple, no power draw) vs green monopropellant (Isp ~235 s, more complex, heater power) vs electric propulsion (Isp ~1500 s, needs significant power, long burn times).

Step 2: Trade — Choose Thruster Type
OptionIsp (s)Propellant Mass NeededProsCons
Cold gas (N₂)65~18.0 kg (impossible — exceeds total mass)Simple, safe, no powerIsp too low for this Δv; needs huge tank
Cold gas (butane)72~14.5 kg (still too much)Self-pressurizing, stores as liquidStill very heavy

Xenon Hall effect1500~0.08 kgMinimal propellant mass100 W+ power, long burn times, complex PPU

For a 6U cubesat, green monopropellant (LMP-103S, ~235 s Isp) is the pragmatic choice — modest propellant mass (~0.65 kg), reasonable heater power (~10–20 W for thruster warm-up), and flight-proven hardware exists in small form factors. Electric propulsion is attractive for the propellant mass savings but its power requirement (100+ W) would dominate the EPS budget.

Step 3: Calculate Propellant Mass

Using the rocket equation to solve for propellant mass:

mp = mf × (eΔv / (Isp × g₀) − 1)

With mf = 8 kg, Isp = 235 s, Δv = 140 m/s:

m₀ = 8 × e140 / (235 × 9.80665) = 8 × e0.0608 = 8 × 1.063 = 8.50 kg

Propellant mass mp = m₀ − mf = 0.50 kg

Adding 30% for residuals and margin: 0.50 × 1.3 = ~0.65 kg loaded propellant.

Step 4: Tank Sizing and System Configuration

LMP-103S has a density of ~1,240 kg/m³. Volume needed:

V = m / ρ = 0.65 kg / 1,240 kg/m³ = 0.00052 m³ = 0.52 L

A spherical tank with a 20% ullage volume: total internal volume ~0.62 L. Radius of a sphere:

r = (3V / 4π)1/3 = (3 × 0.00062 / 4π)1/3 ≈ 0.053 m = 53 mm

A 106 mm diameter titanium tank fits comfortably in a 6U chassis (typically 100 × 200 × 300 mm). Add a thruster, filter, latching valve, fill/drain valve, and a heater with temperature sensor — the total propulsion subsystem dry mass including mounting bracket is ~0.5–0.8 kg.

The configuration: Blowdown feed, single thruster, 1/4″ tubing, surface-tension PMD tank, 0.65 kg LMP-103S, 1 N thruster.

Step 5: Convergence

The preliminary design closes: 0.65 kg propellant, 0.5 kg dry propulsion mass, total wet mass ~1.15 kg within a 6U cubesat (~10–12 kg total).

Now iterate:

  • Could you use electric propulsion if the spacecraft has 150 W of array power and the mission can tolerate months-long burns? Propellant mass drops to ~0.08 kg, freeing mass for payload, but the PPU and tankage adds cost and complexity.
  • Could cold gas work for a reduced mission? If the Δv requirement were only 30 m/s (station-keeping only, no deorbit), cold gas would need ~0.45 kg — viable and much simpler.
  • Is the Isp estimate optimistic? If actual Isp is 220 s instead of 235 s, propellant mass increases to ~0.57 kg (still within margin).
  • Could you share propellant with an ADCS cold-gas system? Some missions use a single propellant tank for both propulsion and attitude control, saving system mass.

Each iteration tightens the design. The converged answer is never the first one — it's the result of working the trades against the real constraints.
