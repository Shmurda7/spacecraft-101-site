Communications (COMMS) Subsystem

The Communications (COMMS) subsystem is the spacecraft's link to the ground. It receives commands, transmits telemetry and science data, and may support cross-link communication between spacecraft. Designing a COMMS subsystem starts from the mission's data requirements — how much data needs to come down, how often you can talk to the ground, and how much power, mass, and money you have to spend. Everything else follows from there.

★ The Fundamentals

The COMMS subsystem is the only connection between the spacecraft and the mission team. If the link fails, the spacecraft is unreachable — no commands can be sent, no data can be recovered. Every COMMS design starts with a simple question: how much data do I need to get down, and how often can I talk to the ground?

The sections below give you the reference material — frequency bands, modulation schemes, antenna types, link budgets, ground stations. But if you're designing a comms system from scratch, start with Section 7 — it walks through a complete example (a cubesat measuring radiation in the Van Allen Belt) step by step, showing how each decision flows from the data requirement.

Every COMMS subsystem is built from six fundamental elements:

ElementWhat it does
RF Front-EndGenerates and receives the radio-frequency carrier. Frequency band, modulation, and output power are determined here.
Antenna SystemConverts electrical signals to electromagnetic waves and vice versa. Gain, beamwidth, polarization, and pointing mechanism define coverage.
TransmitterAmplifies the modulated signal to the power level needed to close the link. Power amplifier efficiency directly impacts the spacecraft power budget.
ReceiverCaptures and demodulates the weak ground signal. Sensitivity, noise figure, and dynamic range determine the minimum usable signal.
Modem & CodingModulates the data onto the carrier and applies error-correcting codes. Determines spectral efficiency and link robustness.
Ground Segment InterfaceThe ground station network, antenna tracking, Doppler compensation, and protocol stack that make the space-to-ground link usable.

These elements work together: the modem produces a modulated signal, the transmitter amplifies it, and the antenna radiates it to the ground station — with every stage subject to frequency allocation, power limits, and link budget constraints.

1. RF Fundamentals

Radio-frequency fundamentals underpin every COMMS design. Frequency band choice determines antenna size, atmospheric loss, and data rate capability. Modulation and coding determine how efficiently you use the bandwidth and how robust the link is to noise. The key insight: there is no direct conversion from frequency to data rate — the relationship depends entirely on how data is modulated onto the carrier wave.

Frequency Bands

Lower frequencies penetrate atmosphere better but offer less bandwidth. Higher frequencies offer more bandwidth but suffer from atmospheric attenuation and rain fade. The band you choose constrains antenna size, available hardware, and regulatory licensing.

Modulation Schemes

A carrier wave oscillating at some frequency can carry data by varying one or more of its properties: amplitude (ASK), frequency (FSK), or phase (PSK). The number of distinct states per symbol determines spectral efficiency in bits per second per Hertz (bps/Hz). Higher-order modulations pack more bits per symbol but require a higher signal-to-noise ratio to maintain the same bit error rate.

SchemeEfficiency (bps/Hz)ComplexityUsed For
BPSK1LowTT&C, deep space (lowest Eb/N₀ required)
QPSK / OQPSK2LowMost common for telemetry and medium-rate downlink
8-PSK3MediumHigher-rate downlink with good efficiency
16-APSK / 32-APSK4–5HighHigh-rate downlink (Ka-band), DVB-S2 standards
GMSK~1.5MediumUHF and S-band with good spectral containment

Spectral efficiency is the ratio of data rate to bandwidth. For example, QPSK encodes 2 bits per symbol, so a 1 MHz channel can carry ~2 Mbps (before coding overhead). Pulse-shaping roll-off and forward error correction both add bandwidth overhead — typically 20–50%.

Error-Correcting Codes

Forward error correction (FEC) adds redundant bits to the transmitted stream so the receiver can correct errors without retransmission. Coding gain is the reduction in required Eb/N₀ for the same BER — a 7 dB coding gain means you can transmit at 1/5 the power for the same link quality.

CodeCoding GainComplexityNotes
Convolutional (r=1/2, k=7)~5 dBLowHeritage code; often concatenated with Reed-Solomon
Reed-Solomon (255,223)~2 dBMediumBlock code; good for burst errors
Turbo codes~7–8 dBHighCCSDS standard; near Shannon-limit performance
LDPC (Low-Density Parity Check)~7–9 dBHighCCSDS standard; modern deep space missions; lower decoding complexity

2. Antennas

The antenna converts electrical signals into radiated electromagnetic waves and vice versa. Antenna design starts with Maxwell's equations — the four laws that describe how electric and magnetic fields are generated and interact. Understanding these fundamentals is essential because antenna performance directly determines whether your link closes, and every parameter (gain, directivity, efficiency, polarization, radiation pattern) involves trade-offs.

Antenna Theory — Maxwell's Equations

All antenna behavior is derived from four physical laws:

LawWhat it means
Gauss's Law for ElectricityElectric charges produce electric fields. The net electric flux through a closed surface is proportional to the charge enclosed.
Gauss's Law for MagnetismThere are no magnetic monopoles. Magnetic field lines form closed loops — they always return to their source.
Faraday's Law of InductionA changing magnetic field induces an electric field. This is how a receiving antenna works: the passing EM wave's magnetic field induces a current in the antenna conductor.
Ampere-Maxwell LawA changing electric field (or a current) produces a magnetic field. This is how a transmitting antenna works: an alternating current in the conductor generates a radiating magnetic field.

Together, these laws describe a self-sustaining process: a changing electric field generates a magnetic field, and a changing magnetic field generates an electric field — producing a wave that propagates outward through space at the speed of light. This is how every antenna works.

Key Antenna Parameters
ParameterWhat It Tells You
Gain (dBi)The ratio of power radiated in a specific direction compared to an isotropic antenna (which radiates equally in all directions). Gain = Directivity × Efficiency. Always given in dBi (dB relative to isotropic). A higher gain antenna focuses the beam more tightly.
DirectivityA measure of how focused the radiation pattern is, regardless of losses. Directivity is what you'd get if the antenna were perfectly efficient. Real gain = directivity × efficiency.
EfficiencyRatio of radiated power to input power. Loss comes from three main sources: reflection loss (impedance mismatch between the antenna and the feed line — like a resistance mismatch for AC signals), conductor loss (energy lost as heat when RF current flows through the metal; skin effect concentrates current near the conductor surface at higher frequencies, increasing effective resistance), and dielectric loss (the PCB substrate or insulating material absorbs some RF power as heat).
PolarizationThe orientation of the electric field vector in the propagating wave. If the transmit and receive antennas have mismatched polarization, you get significant loss — complete mismatch means no connection.
Beamwidth (HPBW)The angular width of the main lobe where power drops to half (−3 dB). Tells you how precisely the antenna must be pointed. Beamwidth is not coverage — coverage requires accounting for the full pattern including side lobes.
VSWR / Return LossHow well the antenna impedance matches the feed line. VSWR < 2:1 is typical (meaning < 10% of power is reflected back).
Polarization Deep Dive

Polarization determines how the electric field of the radiated wave behaves:

TypeBehaviorProsCons
LinearElectric field oscillates in a single plane (vertical or horizontal)Direct and concentrated; stable and long-range; simple, inexpensive, easy to repairRequires perfect alignment between transmit and receive antennas; any rotation causes loss
CircularElectric and magnetic field vectors are 90° out of phase, causing the resultant field vector to rotate (right-hand or left-hand)Immune to interference; works regardless of spacecraft attitude; ideal for unpredictable orientationsReduced range compared to linear; more complex antenna feed design
EllipticalThe two field components have different magnitudes, creating an elliptical trajectoryFlexible signal transmission; improved reception in complex environmentsVery complex to design and analyze

Circular polarization is created by feeding the antenna with two orthogonal feed ports, with a 90° phase offset between them. The simplest way to create the 90° offset is to make one feed line a quarter-wavelength longer than the other. Most spacecraft use circular polarization because the attitude can change unpredictably, and circular polarization doesn't care about orientation.

Radiation Patterns

A radiation pattern describes how an antenna radiates power into space:

Pattern TypeDescription
Isotropic (theoretical)Equal radiation in all directions. Used as the baseline reference for gain measurements.
OmnidirectionalRadiates equally in a plane (e.g., a dipole radiates like a doughnut). Useful when spacecraft attitude is unknown.
DirectionalFocuses energy into a narrow beam (parabolic dish, horn, patch array). Higher gain but needs accurate pointing.

Key terms: Main lobe = the strongest beam. Side lobes = unwanted beams at other angles (these can cause interference). Front-to-back ratio = difference between forward and backward radiation (higher is better).

Patch Antenna Design (Example)

The patch (microstrip) antenna is the workhorse of small spacecraft. Made of a highly conductive metal (copper) patch of length L and width W on a dielectric substrate of thickness h with permittivity εr:

  • The thickness h must be much smaller than the wavelength, but not smaller than λ/40 or efficiency degrades.
  • The resonant frequency is determined by the patch length L, which should equal half a wavelength within the dielectric: L = λ / (2√εr).
  • The width W controls the input impedance. For a square patch, input impedance is ~300 Ω. Increasing W reduces impedance — reaching 50 Ω often requires a very wide patch (lots of space).
  • Larger width also increases bandwidth.
  • The radiation pattern broadens as the substrate thickness increases.

4. Link Analysis

The link budget is the fundamental calculation that proves the communication link will work. It accounts for every gain and loss between the transmitter and receiver — transmit power, antenna gains, free-space path loss, atmospheric effects, pointing error, and implementation margin. The result is compared against the required signal quality for your modulation and coding scheme to determine whether the link has positive margin.

The Link Budget Equation

The received power at the ground station is:

Prx = EIRP + Grx − FSL − Lenv − Lpoint

Where:

TermMeaning
EIRP (Effective Isotropic Radiated Power)The realistic amount of power radiated from the antenna. EIRP = PT + GT − Losssys. PT is the transmitter power, GT is the transmit antenna gain, Losssys includes cable loss, connector loss, and noise temperature effects.
GrxGain of the receiving antenna (ground station).
FSL (Free-Space Path Loss)FSL = (4πR/λ)² where R is distance and λ is wavelength. The signal spreads out as it travels — power drops with the square of distance. This is the dominant loss in any space link.
LenvAtmospheric and environmental losses: oxygen absorption, water vapor, rain fade (significant above 10 GHz), polarization mismatch.
LpointPointing loss — the reduction in gain when the spacecraft or ground antenna isn't perfectly aligned. For omni antennas, pointing loss is negligible.
From Received Power to Link Quality

Received power alone doesn't tell you if the link works — you need the signal-to-noise ratio. The chain of calculations:

StepCalculationPurpose
1. G/T (receiver sensitivity)G/T = Grx − TsysGain-to-noise-temperature ratio. Tsys is the system noise temperature (sum of receiver noise, antenna noise, sky noise, and ground noise — all converted through the noise temperature equation). A higher G/T means a more sensitive receiver.
2. C/N₀ (carrier-to-noise density)C/N₀ = EIRP + G/T − Lossenv − kThe carrier power divided by the noise power spectral density. k = Boltzmann's constant (−228.6 dBW/Hz·K).
3. Eb/N₀ (energy per bit)Eb/N₀ = C/N₀ − RbEnergy per bit divided by noise density. Rb is the bit rate. This is the fundamental metric — it tells you how much noise pollution each bit has to fight through.
4. BER (bit error rate)BER = f(Eb/N₀, modulation, coding)The probability that a single bit is wrong. Each modulation scheme has a known BER vs Eb/N₀ curve. For QPSK at Eb/N₀ = 9 dB, the BER is ~10⁻³ (1 error per 1000 bits). Most missions require BER between 10⁻⁵ and 10⁻⁷ for telemetry, and 10⁻⁹ for commanding.
Noise Temperature

Noise temperature is how RF engineers quantify noise. Every component in the signal path adds noise — the antenna sees sky noise and ground noise, the LNA contributes its own noise, the cables add noise. All these are summed into a system noise temperature (Tsys) using the Friis formula for cascaded stages. A lower Tsys = less noise = better sensitivity. Typical spacecraft receive chains have Tsys of 100–500 K depending on frequency band and ground station quality.

Complete Link Budget — Every Variable Explained

A link budget accounts for every gain and loss from the transmitter output to the receiver input. Here is each term broken down, with its equation and the physical meaning behind it.

1. Transmit Side

EIRP (Effective Isotropic Radiated Power) — the power that would have to be fed into an isotropic antenna to get the same signal strength in the direction of the receiver.

EIRP = PT + GT − Lfeed
TermMeaningTypical Value
PTTransmitter output power at the RF connector (dBm or dBW). For a cubesat, typically 20–30 dBm (0.1–1 W). Power amplifiers are specified at 1 dB compression point — don't operate above it or distortion rises sharply.27 dBm (0.5 W)
GTTransmit antenna gain (dBi). A patch antenna might give 5 dBi; a helix gives 10–15 dBi; a dish gives 20–50 dBi. The gain is always referenced to an isotropic radiator. Gain is not free — higher gain means a narrower beam, which means tighter pointing requirements.5 dBi (patch)
LfeedFeed and cable losses between the transmitter and the antenna (dB). Coaxial cable at S-band can lose 0.5–2 dB. Connector interfaces add 0.1–0.3 dB each. In space, these losses are permanent — there is no way to fix a loose connector after launch.1–3 dB

2. Path Loss

Free-Space Path Loss (FSL) — the signal spreads out as it travels, like ripples from a stone dropped in water. The power density drops with the square of distance.

FSL = 32.4 + 20·log10(f) + 20·log10(d)
TermMeaning
fFrequency in MHz. Higher frequencies have shorter wavelengths and therefore higher path loss at the same physical distance. Doubling the frequency adds 6 dB of path loss.
dDistance in km. For LEO, this is the slant range from spacecraft to ground station at the horizon, not the altitude. At 400 km altitude with a 10° elevation mask, slant range is ~1,200 km. Deep space links can have distances measured in AU (1 AU = 150 million km), where FSL exceeds 250 dB.

The 32.4 constant comes from (4π/c) expressed in dB, where c = 3×10⁸ m/s. The full form is FSL = (4πd/λ)² — the square of the ratio of distance to wavelength.

Atmospheric Loss (Latm) — the atmosphere absorbs RF energy. The absorption comes from oxygen (O₂) and water vapor (H₂O) molecules, each with resonant absorption lines at specific frequencies.

FrequencyZenith Loss5° Elevation LossNote
VHF/UHFnegligible<0.5 dBAtmosphere is essentially transparent below 1 GHz
S-band (2.4 GHz)~0.03 dB~0.3 dBVery low absorption — one reason S-band is so popular
X-band (8.4 GHz)~0.04 dB~0.5 dBStill manageable; starts to become significant below 10° elevation
Ka-band (32 GHz)~0.1 dB~1–3 dBRain fade becomes a major factor; need site diversity or fade mitigation
V-band (60 GHz)~15 dBextremeO₂ resonance at 60 GHz; used only for inter-satellite links

Atmospheric loss varies with elevation angle because the signal passes through more atmosphere at low elevation. The relationship is approximately: Latm(θ) = Lzenith / sin(θ). Most link budgets use a fixed value for a minimum elevation angle (typically 5–10°).

Polarization Mismatch Loss — when the transmit and receive antennas have different polarizations, some signal power is lost. The loss is:

Lpol = −20·log10(|cos(Δθ)|)

where Δθ is the angle between the transmitted and received polarization vectors. For circular-to-circular with same sense (RHCP→RHCP), loss is near 0 dB. For circular-to-linear, loss is ~3 dB (the circular signal has equal power in both linear components, so half is lost). For orthogonal linear (vertical→horizontal), loss is infinite — no signal gets through. This is why spacecraft almost always use circular polarization: it doesn't matter how the spacecraft tumbles, the ground can still receive the signal, albeit with some rotation loss.

3. Receive Side

Ground Station Gain (Grx) — the receiving antenna's gain. For a parabolic dish, gain is related to the antenna aperture area:

G = η · (πD/λ)²

where η is the aperture efficiency (typically 50–70%), D is the dish diameter, and λ is the wavelength. A 3 m dish at S-band has ~30 dBi of gain. Antenna gain is reciprocal — the same antenna has the same gain whether transmitting or receiving.

Pointing Loss (Lpoint) — when the antenna beam is not perfectly aligned, the received signal is reduced. For a Gaussian beam approximation:

Lpoint = 12 · (θerr / θHPBW)²

where θerr is the pointing error and θHPBW is the half-power beamwidth. For an omnidirectional antenna, pointing loss is essentially zero. For a high-gain dish, even a fraction of a degree of error can cause several dB of loss. Typical values: 0.5–2 dB for moderate pointing accuracy.

4. System Noise Temperature

Noise temperature is how RF engineers quantify noise power. The total system noise temperature is the sum of all noise sources, referenced to the receiver input:

Tsys = Tant + TLNA + Tcable

Each term represents the noise contributed by a different part of the system:

ComponentTypical ValueWhat It Is
Tant (Antenna noise)10–150 KNoise picked up by the antenna from the sky, ground, and cosmic background. At S-band looking at cold sky, ~10–30 K. Looking at the warm Earth (290 K), much higher. The antenna noise temperature varies with pointing direction and frequency.
TLNA (LNA noise)30–100 KThe Low-Noise Amplifier adds noise of its own. LNA noise figure (NF) is related to noise temperature by: T = 290 · (10NF/10 − 1). A 0.5 dB NF LNA has T ≈ 35 K. This is the first amplifier in the chain, so its noise figure dominates the receiver noise budget.
Tcable (Cable loss noise)variableA lossy cable between the antenna and LNA adds noise proportional to its physical temperature. For a cable with loss L (dB) at physical temperature T0: Tcable = T0 · (10L/10 − 1). This is why the LNA should be placed as close to the antenna as possible — any loss before the LNA directly degrades the system noise temperature.

The Friis formula for cascaded noise is: Tsys = T1 + T2/G1 + T3/(G1G2) + ... where Gi is the gain of the i-th stage. This shows why the first-stage LNA gain is critical — it suppresses the noise of later stages. A typical LNA has 20–30 dB of gain, making the noise of everything after it negligible.

5. From C/N₀ to Eb/N₀

The carrier-to-noise density ratio (C/N₀) tells you the signal quality per Hertz of bandwidth. To get the energy per bit, divide by the data rate:

Eb/N₀ = C/N₀ − 10·log10(Rb)

where Rb is the bit rate in bps. Eb/N₀ is the fundamental metric because it normalizes for data rate — it tells you how much signal energy each bit gets relative to the noise floor. A higher data rate spreads the same energy over more bits, so each bit gets less energy. This is the fundamental trade: higher data rate requires either more transmit power, a higher-gain antenna, or a lower-noise receiver.

6. Bit Error Rate and Required Eb/N₀

Each modulation scheme has a known relationship between Eb/N₀ and bit error rate (BER). For coherent BPSK/QPSK:

BER = ½ · erfc(√(Eb/N₀))

where erfc is the complementary error function. Required Eb/N₀ for common modulation/coding combinations:

ModulationBER = 10⁻³BER = 10⁻⁵BER = 10⁻⁷
BPSK6.8 dB9.6 dB11.3 dB
QPSK6.8 dB9.6 dB11.3 dB
8-PSK10.5 dB13.4 dB15.2 dB
16-QAM12.4 dB15.5 dB17.4 dB

Forward error correction (FEC) coding improves the effective required Eb/N₀ by the coding gain. A rate-1/2 convolutional code gives ~5 dB of coding gain. Turbo codes and LDPC codes can give 7–9 dB, approaching the Shannon limit (the theoretical minimum Eb/N₀ for error-free communication in additive white Gaussian noise).

Link Margin

Link margin = (actual Eb/N₀) − (required Eb/N₀ for target BER). Positive margin means the link works. Typical guidelines: 3 dB minimum for TT&C links, 5–10 dB for payload data downlinks. Negative margin forces a trade — increase transmitter power, use a higher-gain antenna, improve the modulation/coding scheme, reduce data rate, or upgrade the ground station.

Implementation margin accounts for uncertainties: hardware performance not matching datasheets, imperfect pointing, aging components, and modeling errors. A typical implementation margin is 2–3 dB. Always include it.

★ The Process

Responsible Engineers: RF Engineer, Systems Engineer, Communications Engineer

Users: Systems Engineers, Operations Engineers, Flight Software Engineers, Ground Station Operators

Designing a communications subsystem begins with the mission's data requirement — how much data needs to come down, how often you can talk to the ground, and how much power, mass, and money you have available. Everything else follows from there.

The typical design workflow:

  1. Determine the data requirement — science data volume per orbit, housekeeping telemetry, ground contact schedule
  2. Choose the modulation scheme — spectral efficiency vs. Eb/N₀ trade; BPSK, QPSK, or higher-order
  3. Select the frequency band — driven by data rate, antenna size constraints, and regulatory environment
  4. Pick polarization and antenna type — circular vs. linear; patch, helix, dish, or phased array
  5. Build the link budget — verify that received power and Eb/N₀ meet the required bit error rate with margin
  6. Iterate and converge — revisit each choice as trades tighten mass, power, cost, and performance

A complete step-by-step walkthrough is in the Design Walkthrough — Worked Example below.

Design Walkthrough — Worked Example

This section walks through designing a COMMS subsystem from scratch using a concrete example. Every decision flows from the data requirement, and each step references the relevant reference section above. The design is iterative — expect to loop back through the steps as choices in one area constrain options in another.

Step 1: Determine the Data Requirement

Scenario: A cubesat measures radiation across the Van Allen Belt. It captures 10 kB/s of science data for 20 minutes per orbit, plus 0.5 kB/s of housekeeping telemetry for the full 90-minute orbit.

Data TypeRateDurationTotal Per Orbit
Science data10 kB/s20 min10,000 B/s × 1,200 s = 12 MB
Housekeeping telemetry0.5 kB/s90 min500 B/s × 5,400 s = 2.7 MB
Total14.7 MB per orbit

Now decide how often to downlink. Say you have 1 ground contact per orbit with a 3-minute access window (determined by your ground station locations and orbital geometry). The required downlink data rate is:

14,700,000 bytes / 180 seconds = 81.67 kBps

This is your minimum data rate — every other choice in the comms design must support at least this rate.

The ground access time is purely determined by ground station location and orbital geometry — how long the spacecraft is within the ground station antenna's field of view. If you can afford more ground stations or a higher-gain ground antenna, your access window and data rate per pass change.

Step 2: Choose the Modulation Scheme

Modulation determines how many bits you can squeeze through each Hertz of bandwidth. For simplicity, start with Phase Shift Keying (PSK):

SchemeSpectral EfficiencyBit Rate in 1 MHzComplexity
BPSK1 bps/Hz1 MbpsLowest
QPSK2 bps/Hz2 MbpsLow
8-PSK3 bps/Hz3 MbpsMedium

Our required data rate is 81.67 kBps = 653 kbps. With BPSK (1 bps/Hz):

653 kbps / 1 bps/Hz = ~653 kHz of bandwidth needed

Adding pulse-shaping roll-off and forward error correction overhead (typically 20–50% extra bandwidth), the total required bandwidth is roughly 800 kHz to 1 MHz. A practical choice is allocating 200 kHz and using a higher-gain antenna or better coding to close the link within that narrow band.

When conducting a modulation trade study, the key parameters are: complexity (more complex = better performance but harder to implement), cost (parts and development), schedule (time to develop and test), and power draw (more complex systems draw more power).

Step 3: Select the Frequency Band

Band selection depends on the data rate requirement, available hardware, regulatory constraints, and the mission orbit. For a low-altitude cubesat (~400 km) that is power, mass, and size constrained:

BandTypical Data RateAntenna Size for Given GainFit for Our Example
UHF (300–1000 MHz)~10–100 kbpsLargeMaybe — low rate, simple hardware, but may not support 653 kbps
S-band (2–4 GHz)~1–10 MbpsSmall to mediumGood fit — plenty of headroom, good hardware availability, moderate atmosphere loss
X-band (8–12 GHz)~10–100+ MbpsSmallOverkill for our data rate, more expensive, more atmospheric loss

For this example, S-band (2–4 GHz) is the pragmatic choice — it has ample bandwidth for our 653 kbps requirement, mature and affordable COTS hardware exists, and the atmospheric loss is manageable even at low elevation angles.

Step 4: Choose Polarization and Antenna Type

For a small spacecraft where attitude can change unpredictably, circular polarization is the standard choice — it doesn't care about orientation. A complete polarization mismatch means no connection, and linear polarization requires precise alignment that a tumbling or slewing spacecraft can't guarantee.

For the antenna type, the trade depends on the mission parameters:

CriterionOur ValuesImplication
Altitude~400 km (LEO)Short range, low path loss — don't need a high-gain antenna
Power, mass, sizeSeverely constrainedSmall, lightweight, low-profile antenna needed
Data rate~653 kbpsReadily achievable with S-band and a modest-gain antenna

A patch (microstrip) antenna is the best fit — low profile, lightweight, circularly polarized, and 3–9 dBi of gain that comfortably closes our link. For a higher-rate mission at X-band, a parabolic dish or phased array would be justified.

With the preliminary design choices made (S-band, QPSK, 653 kbps, patch antenna, circular polarization), the next step is to verify the link will close by building a preliminary link budget:

Step 6: Iterate and Converge

With the budget closed, you have a baseline design: S-band, QPSK modulation, circular polarization, patch antenna, 0.5 W transmitter, with ~1 Mbps capacity. Now iterate through the trades:

  • Could you use a lower-power transmitter and save 0.3 W of spacecraft power? Re-run the budget with 0.2 W.
  • Could you use a simpler ground station (less gain) and save ground segment cost? Re-run the budget with a 5 dBi ground antenna.
  • Is the BER requirement too tight? If the mission can tolerate 10⁻⁴ instead of 10⁻⁵, the required Eb/N₀ drops by ~1 dB, giving more margin.

Each iteration tightens the design. The final design is never the first one — it's the converged result of 5–10 passes through the trades, with each decision checked against the others.
