Systems Engineering

Systems engineering is the interdisciplinary process that ties every spacecraft subsystem together — translating mission goals into a real, flyable vehicle. It covers the budgets that constrain every design, the requirements that define what must be built, the verification proving it works, the trade studies that balance competing options, the concept of operations describing how it will be used, and the interface control documents that keep everyone aligned.

Before You Dive In — The Big Picture

Systems engineering is what makes a spacecraft more than a pile of components. It ensures that the power subsystem delivers enough energy, the comms link closes with margin, the structure stays within mass, and every interface between subsystems agrees on voltage levels, data formats, connector pinouts, and protocol timing.

Every spacecraft program is built on six systems engineering foundations:

FoundationWhat it does
BudgetsTrack every constrained resource — mass, power, data rate, link margin, pointing error, delta-v — against allocations. A budget is a living spreadsheet that tells you whether the spacecraft still works as the design evolves.
RequirementsThe contract between the mission and the design. Requirements cascade from mission objectives → system → subsystem → component, each level adding specificity and verifiability.
Verification & ValidationProving that the spacecraft meets its requirements. Verification (did we build it right?) uses test, analysis, inspection, or demonstration. Validation (did we build the right thing?) asks whether the system satisfies the mission need.
Trade SpacesEvery design decision involves trading competing options against cost, schedule, performance, and risk. Trade studies provide a structured framework to compare and decide.
CONOPSThe story of how the spacecraft operates from launch through end of life — describing each phase, who does what, data flows, timelines, and critical events.
ICDsThe official record of every interface between subsystems, between spacecraft and launch vehicle, and between spacecraft and ground. An ICD leaves no ambiguity about pin assignments, protocol timing, or mechanical envelope.

These six foundations interact constantly: a CONOPS drives requirements, requirements set budget targets, budgets constrain trade studies, and ICDs document the results — all verified before launch.

1. Budgets

Budgets are the tracking mechanism that ensures every spacecraft resource stays within what's available. Each budget is a top-down allocation from system-level targets down to subsystem and component limits, tracked throughout the design lifecycle with margin held at each level.

Mass Budget
CategoryDescription
Subsystem massDry mass of each subsystem (CDH, EPS, ADCS, COMMS, Thermal, Propulsion, Structures, Payload)
Harness massCabling between subsystems, including connectors and brackets
Propellant massFuel + oxidizer for orbit insertion, station-keeping, disposal
Margin philosophyGrowth margin held at each design phase (typical: 20% at PDR, 10% at CDR, 5% at manufacturing)
Dry vs wet massDry mass = spacecraft without propellant; wet mass = dry + propellant (used for launcher compatibility)
Power Budget
CategoryDescription
Subsystem power drawPeak and average power consumption per subsystem in each operational mode
Power generationSolar array output at beginning of life (BOL) and end of life (EOL), accounting for radiation degradation and seasonal variation
Energy storageBattery capacity, depth of discharge (DoD), eclipse duration, cycle life
Power modesSun-point, eclipse, safe hold, thrusting, payload operation — each with a distinct load profile
MarginTypical 15–25% margin on generation and storage at system level, 10–15% on consumption
Link Budget
CategoryDescription
Uplink (command)Ground transmitter power → space loss → antenna gains → receiver sensitivity → data rate → margin
Downlink (telemetry)Spacecraft transmitter power → space loss → antenna gains → ground receiver sensitivity → data rate → margin
Cross-linkRelative geometry, range, and frequency plan between spacecraft in a constellation
Fade marginsAtmospheric attenuation, rain fade (for low elevation passes), polarization mismatch, pointing losses
Bit error rateRequired BER (typically 10⁻⁵ to 10⁻⁷ for telemetry, 10⁻⁹ for commanding) drives coding scheme and required Eb/N₀
Data Budget
CategoryDescription
Science data volumeData generated per orbit/observation cycle, compression ratio, storage requirement
Telemetry generationHousekeeping telemetry rate — temperature, voltage, current, status words per second per subsystem
Ground contact windowPass duration, frequency of passes, data rate achievable at maximum slant range
Onboard storage sizingNVM needed to store data between downlink opportunities, including margin for contingency
Command volumeUplink capacity for time-tagged commands, software patches, and database updates
Other Budgets
BudgetDescription
Pointing budgetAllocation of pointing error among sensors, actuators, structural misalignment, thermal distortion, and control accuracy
Delta-v budgetTotal velocity change needed for mission phases (insertion, station-keeping, disposal) + margins + dispersions
Timing budgetEnd-to-end latency from sensor measurement to onboard response, ground-in-the-loop delay, and on-time accuracy
Thermal budgetHeat generated by each unit, radiator area, heater power, and allowable temperature ranges per component

2. Requirements

Requirements define what the spacecraft must do, how well it must do it, and under what conditions. They flow from the mission statement down through system, subsystem, and component levels, with traceability maintained throughout the program.

Requirements Hierarchy
LevelExample
Mission-level"Measure ocean color at 500 m GSD over 90% of global oceans every 2 days for 5 years"
System-level"The spacecraft shall provide ≤300 W orbit-average power to payload"
Subsystem-level"The EPS shall deliver 28 V ± 2% regulated bus at 150 W continuous"
Component-level"The battery shall provide 100 Ah at 28 V with ≤30% DoD over 30,000 cycles"
Types of Requirements
TypeDescription
FunctionalWhat the system must do (e.g., "The CDH shall execute time-tagged commands with 1 ms resolution")
PerformanceHow well it must do it (e.g., "The pointing control system shall maintain 0.05° accuracy, 3σ")
InterfaceHow it connects to other systems (e.g., "The OBC shall communicate with the EPS via CAN bus at 500 kbps")
EnvironmentalConditions it must survive (e.g., "All electronics shall operate after 50 krad TID")
OperationalHow it is used (e.g., "The spacecraft shall support autonomous safe hold for 72 hours without ground contact")
ConstraintDesign limits (e.g., "Total spacecraft dry mass shall not exceed 400 kg")
Good Requirements (SMART)
CriterionMeaning
SpecificOne clear subject and one testable condition — avoid "and," "or," compound statements
MeasurableQuantifiable with a pass/fail criterion (time, accuracy, probability, etc.)
AttainableFeasible within budget, schedule, and technology readiness
RelevantDirectly supports a higher-level requirement or mission need
TraceableLinked to a parent requirement and to its verification method
Requirements Management
ActivityDescription
ElicitationGathering needs from stakeholders — science team, operations, propulsion, bus engineers, launch provider
AllocationDistributing system requirements to subsystems with clear responsibility and numeric targets
TraceabilityParent-child links up and down the hierarchy; every requirement justified and verifiable
Change controlFormal process for adding, modifying, or deleting requirements — each change assessed for cost/schedule/performance impact

3. Verification & Validation

Verification confirms the spacecraft was built to its requirements (did we build it right?). Validation confirms it meets the mission need (did we build the right thing?). Both are planned early and executed through each assembly and test phase.

Verification Methods
MethodWhen UsedExample
TestFunctional performance measured under simulated or flight-like conditionsThermal vacuum test, vibration test, RF link test
AnalysisMathematical modeling or simulation where physical test is impracticalFinite element model for structural margin, orbital debris risk analysis
InspectionVisual or physical check of workmanship, dimensions, or configurationConnector pin verification, torque-strip inspection, fit check
DemonstrationOperational function shown without quantitative measurementDeployment mechanism actuation, software boot sequence
Verification Levels
LevelScope
Component / unit-levelEach box (OBC, power supply, star tracker, valve) tested to its own specification before integration
Subsystem-levelIntegrated units within a subsystem tested together (e.g., CDH + FSW test bench)
System-levelFull spacecraft tested as a single system — functional, environmental, EMC, end-to-end RF
Launch & mission rehearsalEnd-to-end timeline simulation with ground segment, including nominal and contingency scenarios
Verification Planning
DocumentContent
VCD (Verification Cross-Reference Matrix)Every requirement mapped to its verification method, level, and pass/fail criteria
VAP (Verification and Acceptance Plan)Overall strategy — what tests are performed, in what order, by whom, with what facilities
VCR (Verification Compliance Record)Close-out report for each requirement showing the evidence that it was met
Validation Activities
ActivityDescription
End-to-end simulationsRunning mission scenarios with the ground system to confirm operations work as expected
Prototyping & demonstration missionsRisk-reduction flights or engineering models to prove critical technologies before the main build
Stakeholder reviewsScience team and operations team sign off that the spacecraft meets their needs at PDR, CDR, and pre-ship review

4. Trade Spaces

Trade studies are the structured process of comparing design alternatives against weighted criteria — cost, performance, risk, schedule, mass, power. They prevent decisions based on gut feel and provide an auditable record of why choices were made.

Trade Study Process
StepDescription
1. Define the decisionWhat is being traded and what constraints are fixed
2. Identify alternativesList all feasible options, including a baseline/reference
3. Select criteria & weightsCriteria relevant to the decision, weighted by importance (e.g., cost 30%, performance 40%, risk 20%, schedule 10%)
4. Score alternativesEach alternative scored against each criterion (1–5 or 1–10 scale)
5. Sensitivity analysisHow do the rankings change if weights shift? Which alternatives are robust?
6. Recommend & documentSelected option with rationale, key discriminators, and risks carried forward
Common System-Level Trades
TradeOptionsKey Drivers
Bus architectureIntegrated bus with shared subsystems · Separate service module + payload module · Distributed vs centralizedMass, cost, reuse across missions, integration complexity
Propulsion typeCold gas · Monopropellant (hydrazine) · Bipropellant · Electric (ion/Hall) · Green propellantsDelta-v requirement, power available, cost, contamination, schedule
Orbit selectionLEO · SSO · GEO · HEO · L1/L2 · Molniya · TundraMission objective, ground contact frequency, radiation environment, launch cost
Attitude control methodSpin-stabilized · 3-axis stabilized · Gravity-gradient · Solar sailPointing accuracy, agility requirement, power generation, cost
Communication frequency bandUHF · S-band · X-band · Ka-band · OpticalData rate vs atmospheric effects vs antenna size vs power vs regulation
Common Subsystem-Level Trades
TradeOptionsKey Drivers
Solar array typeBody-mounted · Deployable (single/dual/multi-panel) · Concentrator · Flexible blanketPower requirement, orbit, stowed volume, cost, pointing requirement
Battery chemistryLi-ion (highest density) · NiCd (heritage) · NiH₂ (long life, heavy) · LiFePO₄ (safe, less dense)Energy density, cycle life, safety, thermal range, heritage
Star tracker vs sun sensor vs magnetometerAccuracy vs cost vs environment vs processingPointing requirement, sun vs eclipse duration, magnetic field knowledge
Thermal control approachPassive (coatings, MLI, radiators) · Heater-controlled · Active (loop heat pipe, mechanical cooler)Temperature stability requirement, power available, cost, complexity
Structural materialAluminum (conventional) · Aluminum honeycomb · Carbon fiber composite · Titanium (for fittings)Mass vs stiffness vs thermal expansion vs cost vs manufacturing
Cost & Schedule Trades
TradeOptionsKey Drivers
Make vs buyIn-house development · COTS with qualification · Heritage with modifications · Fully customCost, schedule, IP control, system integration risk
Parts quality levelSpace-grade (QML V/Q) · Mil-grade (JANTX) · Automotive (AEC-Q) · Commercial + upscreenReliability vs cost vs lead time vs radiation tolerance
Test philosophyProtoflight · Qualification model + flight model · Full engineering model + flight modelCost vs risk vs heritage vs schedule

5. Concept of Operations (CONOPS)

CONOPS describes how the spacecraft will be operated from launch through end of life. It covers each mission phase, the actors involved, the data flows, and the key decisions or events that transition between phases. CONOPS drives requirements — you can't write good requirements without knowing how the system will be used.

Mission Phases
PhaseActivities
Launch & ascentSpacecraft is powered off (or on battery), inside the fairing, experiencing vibration and acoustic loads. Separation from launcher is the first critical event.
Orbit insertionFirst burn(s) to reach operational orbit. May involve propulsion system checkout, de-spin, Sun acquisition, and deployment of solar arrays and antennas.
CommissioningSubsystem checkout in sequence — EPS, COMMS, CDH, ADCS, Thermal, then payload. Calibration of sensors and instruments. Typically 2–12 weeks.
Nominal operationsScience data collection, payload operation, station-keeping maneuvers, routine downlink passes, command uploads. The longest phase.
Contingency operationsAnomaly response — safe hold entry, fault diagnosis, recovery procedure execution, ground-in-the-loop troubleshooting.
End of lifeDisposal — deorbit burn (LEO) or graveyard orbit (GEO). Passivation of batteries and propulsion tanks. Final telemetry pass.
CONOPS Content
ElementDescription
Operational scenariosNominal timeline (typical orbit), safe hold entry/recovery, eclipse operations, station-keeping, anomaly response
Ground segment interactionPass schedule, contact duration, command latency, telemetry dump strategy, ground station network
Autonomy levelGround commanding vs onboard autonomy — time-tagged command load, autonomous FDIR, target tracking, on-board scheduling
Data flowScience data path (instrument → storage → downlink → ground processing), housekeeping telemetry flow, command path
Operational constraintsSun angle limits, thermal constraints, momentum management, ground station visibility, RF interference zones
CONOPS Timelines
TimelineContent
Single-orbit timelineSun-point, eclipse, payload operation, downlink pass, station-keeping (if any), calibrated against orbit period
Weekly/monthly timelineScience planning cycles, propulsive maneuver scheduling, ground contact scheduling, calibration cycles
Mission-long timelinePhase transitions from launch through disposal, key milestones, seasonal variations (beta angle, eclipses)

6. Interface Control Documents (ICDs)

ICDs are the official, controlled documents that define every interface between two systems. They eliminate ambiguity during integration — if two teams disagree about a pin assignment, protocol timing, or mechanical dimension, the ICD is the authority.

Types of ICDs
TypeBetween
Subsystem-to-subsystem ICDEvery pair of subsystems that share an interface (e.g., CDH ↔ EPS for power and data)
Spacecraft-to-launch-vehicle ICDMechanical envelope, separation system, vibration environment, electrical interface, fairing accommodation, mass properties
Spacecraft-to-ground ICDRF link parameters, modulation, coding, protocol, command & telemetry frame formats, ranging scheme
Internal ICDBetween units within a subsystem (e.g., OBC ↔ memory daughterboard, star tracker ↔ ADCS controller)
ICD Content (Typical)
Interface TypeContent
MechanicalMounting hole pattern, envelope dimensions, connector locations, mass, CG, stiffness, thermal contact conductance
ElectricalSupply voltage, current limits, inrush, ripple, isolation, grounding scheme, power-on sequencing
Data / signalBus type (CAN, SpaceWire, LVDS), data rate, protocol version, message format, timing diagrams, connector pin assignments
RFFrequency, bandwidth, polarization, EIRP, G/T, maximum input power, spurious emissions, connector type
ThermalHeat rejection path, allowable temperature range, heater power, radiator surface finish, MLI cutouts
FluidPropellant type, pressure, flow rate, fitting type, leak rate requirement, material compatibility
ICD Process
StepDescription
DraftDeveloped by the system engineer with input from both interface owners; identifies all interface points and parameters
ReviewReviewed and signed by responsible engineers from both sides of the interface
Version controlEvery change tracked, dated, and approved; deviations require formal waiver
VerificationEach interface parameter verified by test, inspection, or analysis before integration
MaintenanceUpdated when design changes affect an interface; superseded versions archived
ICD Best Practices
  • Every interface parameter has a single owner responsible for its definition.
  • Use tables and diagrams — avoid prose descriptions of pin assignments or timing.
  • Define worst-case limits (min/max), not just nominal values.
  • Include verification method for each parameter (test, analysis, inspection).
  • Keep ICDs at the appropriate level of detail — enough for independent design, not a full schematic.
  • Use controlled distribution — everyone working on the interface must have the current version.
