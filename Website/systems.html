Systems Engineering

Systems engineering is the interdisciplinary process that ties every spacecraft subsystem together — translating mission goals into a real, flyable vehicle. It covers the budgets that constrain every design, the requirements that define what must be built, the verification proving it works, the trade studies that balance competing options, the concept of operations describing how it will be used, and the interface control documents that keep everyone aligned.

★ The Idea

Systems engineering is what makes a spacecraft more than a pile of components. It ensures that the power subsystem delivers enough energy, the comms link closes with margin, the structure stays within mass, and every interface between subsystems agrees on voltage levels, data formats, connector pinouts, and protocol timing.

Every spacecraft program is built on seven systems engineering foundations:

FoundationWhat it does
BudgetsTrack every constrained resource — mass, power, data rate, link margin, pointing error, delta-v — against allocations. A budget is a living spreadsheet that tells you whether the spacecraft still works as the design evolves.
RequirementsThe contract between the mission and the design. Requirements cascade from mission objectives → system → subsystem → component, each level adding specificity and verifiability.
Verification & ValidationProving that the spacecraft meets its requirements. Verification (did we build it right?) uses test, analysis, inspection, or demonstration. Validation (did we build the right thing?) asks whether the system satisfies the mission need.
Trade SpacesEvery design decision involves trading competing options against cost, schedule, performance, and risk. Trade studies provide a structured framework to compare and decide.
CONOPSThe story of how the spacecraft operates from launch through end of life — describing each phase, who does what, data flows, timelines, and critical events.
ICDsThe official record of every interface between subsystems, between spacecraft and launch vehicle, and between spacecraft and ground. An ICD leaves no ambiguity about pin assignments, protocol timing, or mechanical envelope.
Fault ManagementThe detection, isolation, and recovery from anomalies that keep the spacecraft safe and the mission viable. Fault management cross-cuts every other foundation — a budget violation, a requirement miss, or an interface mismatch can all become faults that the system must handle.

These foundations interact constantly: a CONOPS drives requirements, requirements set budget targets, budgets constrain trade studies, ICDs document the results, and fault management protects it all — all verified before launch.

★ The Process

Responsible Engineers: Systems Engineer, Lead Systems Engineer

Users: All subsystem engineers, Mission Assurance, Project Management, Operations

Systems engineering is the thread that ties every subsystem together. The process starts with mission objectives and cascades through requirements, budgets, verification planning, trade studies, CONOPS development, and ICD creation — with continuous iteration at every step.

The typical SE workflow:

  1. Define mission objectives — what must the spacecraft accomplish, in what orbit, for how long
  2. Derive and allocate requirements — mission → system → subsystem → component, each with verifiable criteria
  3. Establish budgets — mass, power, data, pointing, delta-v — allocate to subsystems with margin
  4. Plan verification & validation — define how each requirement will be proven (test, analysis, inspection, demonstration)
  5. Conduct trade studies — compare design alternatives against weighted criteria to make defendable decisions
  6. Develop CONOPS — describe how the spacecraft will be operated in each mission phase
  7. Write ICDs — document every interface between subsystems, launcher, and ground
  8. Define fault management — identify credible faults, define detection thresholds, isolation strategies, and recovery actions for each mission phase

Each step feeds back into the others — a trade study may change a requirement, a CONOPS detail may drive a new budget line. Systems engineering is never a straight line.

1. Budgets

Budgets are the tracking mechanism that ensures every spacecraft resource stays within what's available. Each budget is a top-down allocation from system-level targets down to subsystem and component limits, tracked throughout the design lifecycle with margin held at each level. Budgets begin from the system level — starting with the total resource available an engineer allocates and reallocates to various subsystems based on assumed component specifications.

Mass Budget

The Idea — A mass budget tracks the total mass of all components for a system, enabling distribution of mass allocations across subsystems and verification against requirements.

The Process — Start with the total mass available for the spacecraft (driven by launch vehicle capability). Allocate mass to each subsystem based on heritage, vendor data, or first-order estimates. Carry margin for components in early development (higher margin) and less for non-new components. Mass growth allowance (MGA) accounts for lower design fidelity — as the design matures, additional mass is added based on design changes, and system-level margin is consumed.

Power Budget

The Idea — A power budget determines the total power required and produced for the spacecraft, analyzed across each operational mode (sun-point, eclipse, payload, safe hold, etc.).

The Process — Begin by listing all components and their required voltage and supplied current from datasheets. Power is calculated as:

Power (W) = Voltage (V) × Current (A)

Then, depending on the mode, multiply by the component's on-time per orbit:

On-Time (hr) = Duty Cycle (%) × Orbit Period (hr)

Orbit Average Power (OAP) = Power (W) × On-Time (hr)

Roll up all component draws per mode to get total power consumption. On the generation side, account for:

  • Solar flux at orbit — 1,367 W/m² at 1 AU; scales with 1/R² for interplanetary
  • Eclipse period — no power generated; battery must supply all loads
  • Solar array efficiency — triple-junction GaAs ~30%; only ~30% of available energy is converted
  • Earth reflection (albedo) — some energy reflected from Earth can supplement generation
  • Solar array off-pointing (cosine effect) — arrays not perfectly perpendicular to the sun vector reduce output

Losses to include: harness losses through cabling and connectors, efficiency losses from MPPT and voltage regulation, and battery charge/discharge efficiency.

Activity Analysis (SoC / DoD)

Beyond the steady-state budget, isolate a given activity and evaluate the State of Charge (SoC) or Depth of Discharge (DoD) of the battery. Compare against the battery specification to determine whether the planned DoD is within safe limits and will not degrade the battery over the mission lifetime.

Link Budget

The Idea — A link budget determines whether the quality of a transmitted signal is sufficient to meet mission requirements. It compares the calculated bit error rate (BER) to the mission's required BER to prove the communication link will close.

The Process — Start with the required data rate and work backward: calculate received power from transmitter power, antenna gains, and free-space path loss; convert to carrier-to-noise density (C/N₀) using system noise temperature; then to energy-per-bit (Eb/N₀) using the data rate; and finally to BER using the modulation scheme's Eb/N₀ curve. Compare against the required BER — if margin is insufficient, iterate on power, antenna size, frequency, or modulation.

Prx = EIRP + Grx − FSL − Lenv − Lpoint

C/N₀ = EIRP + G/T − Lossenv − k

Eb/N₀ = C/N₀ − Rb

Data Budget

The Idea — A data budget tracks how much data is generated, stored, and downlinked per orbit, ensuring the onboard storage and ground network can handle the mission's data volume.

The Process — Sum science data and housekeeping telemetry per orbit, determine ground contact frequency and access window duration, then calculate the required downlink data rate. Size onboard storage to hold data between downlink opportunities plus margin for contingencies.

CategoryDescription
Science data volumeData generated per orbit/observation cycle, compression ratio, storage requirement
Telemetry generationHousekeeping telemetry rate — temperature, voltage, current, status words per second per subsystem
Ground contact windowPass duration, frequency of passes, data rate achievable at maximum slant range
Onboard storage sizingNVM needed to store data between downlink opportunities, including margin for contingency
Command volumeUplink capacity for time-tagged commands, software patches, and database updates
Harness Design & Budget

The Idea — The harness (cabling and connectors) carries power, data, and RF signals between every spacecraft component. It must be designed for current capacity, signal integrity, thermal environment, and vibration — while staying within a mass allocation that is easy to underestimate.

The Process — Start with a cable schedule listing every cable: from/to, wire gauge, length, connector type, shield type. Select wire gauge based on current (with derating per NASA-STD-8739.4), voltage drop, and allowable temperature rise. Separate power cables from signal cables to minimize coupling. Define bend radius limits, strain relief, and tie-down intervals. Track total harness mass against allocation in the mass budget.

The Design considerations:

ParameterConsiderations
Wire gauge selectionBased on current draw per pin with derating (typically 60% of rated ampacity for space). Voltage drop across the cable run must stay within the unit's tolerance. Larger gauge for power lines (18–22 AWG), smaller for signals (24–30 AWG).
Connector selectionD-sub (signal), Micro-D (dense signal), circular (power/RF), coax (RF). Specify keying to prevent mis-mating, pin retention, backshell for strain relief, and potting for environmental sealing.
Shielding & groundingSignal cables may require individual or overall shields, grounded at one end (to avoid ground loops). Power return currents return on dedicated wires, not through the structure. Chassis ground connections provide EMC bonding.
Routing & installationPower and signal cables physically separated. Bend radius ≥ 6× cable diameter. Cables tied down at intervals ≤ 150 mm. Margin for harness routing changes during integration (typically 10–15% spare pins and 10% extra cable length).
Other Budgets
BudgetDescription
Pointing budgetAllocation of pointing error among sensors, actuators, structural misalignment, thermal distortion, and control accuracy
Delta-v budgetTotal velocity change needed for mission phases (insertion, station-keeping, disposal) + margins + dispersions
Timing budgetEnd-to-end latency from sensor measurement to onboard response, ground-in-the-loop delay, and on-time accuracy

2. Requirements

Requirements define what the spacecraft must do, how well it must do it, and under what conditions. They flow from the mission statement down through system, subsystem, and component levels, with traceability maintained throughout the program.

Requirements Hierarchy
LevelExample
Mission-level"Measure ocean color at 500 m GSD over 90% of global oceans every 2 days for 5 years"
System-level"The spacecraft shall provide ≤300 W orbit-average power to payload"
Subsystem-level"The EPS shall deliver 28 V ± 2% regulated bus at 150 W continuous"
Component-level"The battery shall provide 100 Ah at 28 V with ≤30% DoD over 30,000 cycles"
Types of Requirements
TypeDescription
FunctionalWhat the system must do (e.g., "The CDH shall execute time-tagged commands with 1 ms resolution")
PerformanceHow well it must do it (e.g., "The pointing control system shall maintain 0.05° accuracy, 3σ")
InterfaceHow it connects to other systems (e.g., "The OBC shall communicate with the EPS via CAN bus at 500 kbps")
EnvironmentalConditions it must survive (e.g., "All electronics shall operate after 50 krad TID")
OperationalHow it is used (e.g., "The spacecraft shall support autonomous safe hold for 72 hours without ground contact")
ConstraintDesign limits (e.g., "Total spacecraft dry mass shall not exceed 400 kg")
Good Requirements (SMART)
CriterionMeaning

MeasurableQuantifiable with a pass/fail criterion (time, accuracy, probability, etc.)
AttainableFeasible within budget, schedule, and technology readiness
RelevantDirectly supports a higher-level requirement or mission need
TraceableLinked to a parent requirement and to its verification method
Requirements Management
ActivityDescription
ElicitationGathering needs from stakeholders — science team, operations, propulsion, bus engineers, launch provider
AllocationDistributing system requirements to subsystems with clear responsibility and numeric targets
TraceabilityParent-child links up and down the hierarchy; every requirement justified and verifiable
Change controlFormal process for adding, modifying, or deleting requirements — each change assessed for cost/schedule/performance impact
Shall vs Should / Technical Requirements Document

Requirements are written in a standard format that makes their mandatory status unambiguous:

KeywordMeaning
ShallMandatory — the requirement must be met. Every "shall" statement is verifiable and tied to a verification method.
ShouldGoal or objective — desirable but not required. "Should" items are tracked but may be waived or deferred.
WillStatement of fact or intent (not a requirement). E.g., "The ground station will use S-band for uplink."
MayPermission or option. E.g., "The operator may override autonomous safe hold via direct command."

The Technical Requirements Document (TRD) is the formal deliverable that collects all system-level requirements. It is baselined at the System Requirements Review (SRR) and placed under change control. Each requirement in the TRD is uniquely identified (e.g., SYS-042), traced to its parent mission objective, and mapped to a verification method in the VCD.

Verification confirms the spacecraft was built to its requirements (did we build it right?). Validation confirms it meets the mission need (did we build the right thing?). Both are planned early and executed through each assembly and test phase.

4. Trade Spaces

Trade studies are the structured process of comparing design alternatives against weighted criteria — cost, performance, risk, schedule, mass, power. They prevent decisions based on gut feel and provide an auditable record of why choices were made.

Trade Study Process
StepDescription
1. Define the decisionWhat is being traded and what constraints are fixed
2. Identify alternativesList all feasible options, including a baseline/reference
3. Select criteria & weightsCriteria relevant to the decision, weighted by importance (e.g., cost 30%, performance 40%, risk 20%, schedule 10%)
4. Score alternativesEach alternative scored against each criterion (1–5 or 1–10 scale)
5. Sensitivity analysisHow do the rankings change if weights shift? Which alternatives are robust?
6. Recommend & documentSelected option with rationale, key discriminators, and risks carried forward

5. Concept of Operations (CONOPS)

CONOPS describes how the spacecraft will be operated from launch through end of life. It covers each mission phase, the actors involved, the data flows, and the key decisions or events that transition between phases. CONOPS drives requirements — you can't write good requirements without knowing how the system will be used.

Mission Phases
PhaseActivities
Launch & ascentSpacecraft is powered off (or on battery), inside the fairing, experiencing vibration and acoustic loads. Separation from launcher is the first critical event.
Orbit insertionFirst burn(s) to reach operational orbit. May involve propulsion system checkout, de-spin, Sun acquisition, and deployment of solar arrays and antennas.
CommissioningSubsystem checkout in sequence — EPS, COMMS, CDH, ADCS, Thermal, then payload. Calibration of sensors and instruments. Typically 2–12 weeks.
Nominal operationsScience data collection, payload operation, station-keeping maneuvers, routine downlink passes, command uploads. The longest phase.
Contingency operationsAnomaly response — safe hold entry, fault diagnosis, recovery procedure execution, ground-in-the-loop troubleshooting.
End of lifeDisposal — deorbit burn (LEO) or graveyard orbit (GEO). Passivation of batteries and propulsion tanks. Final telemetry pass.
CONOPS Content
ElementDescription
Operational scenariosNominal timeline (typical orbit), safe hold entry/recovery, eclipse operations, station-keeping, anomaly response
Ground segment interactionPass schedule, contact duration, command latency, telemetry dump strategy, ground station network
Autonomy levelGround commanding vs onboard autonomy — time-tagged command load, autonomous FDIR, target tracking, on-board scheduling
Data flowScience data path (instrument → storage → downlink → ground processing), housekeeping telemetry flow, command path
Operational constraintsSun angle limits, thermal constraints, momentum management, ground station visibility, RF interference zones
CONOPS Timelines
TimelineContent
Single-orbit timelineSun-point, eclipse, payload operation, downlink pass, station-keeping (if any), calibrated against orbit period
Weekly/monthly timelineScience planning cycles, propulsive maneuver scheduling, ground contact scheduling, calibration cycles
Mission-long timelinePhase transitions from launch through disposal, key milestones, seasonal variations (beta angle, eclipses)
CONOPS vs OpsCon / Document Structure

CONOPS (Concept of Operations) describes the system from the user's perspective — what the spacecraft does, how it is operated, and who interacts with it. OpsCon (Operational Concept) describes the system's internal view — how the system itself behaves to deliver the CONOPS. On a small program the two are often combined; on large programs they are separate documents.

A well-structured CONOPS document covers three areas:

AreaContent
System DefinitionSpacecraft configuration (bus + payload), orbit parameters, ground segment architecture, launch vehicle, mission lifetime, key performance parameters. Establishes the scope and boundaries of the operations concept.
Modes of OperationDefines each spacecraft operational mode: Launch, Safe Hold, Sun-point, Science, Downlink, Maneuver, Commissioning, End-of-Life. For each mode: entry conditions, activities performed, subsystems active, power/data state, transition conditions to other modes.
Operational ScenariosTime-ordered narratives covering Prior to Mission (pre-launch, launch), Mission (commissioning, nominal ops, contingency ops, station-keeping), and Post-Mission (disposal, passivation). Each scenario includes the timeline, actors, data flows, and key decisions.

6. Interface Control Documents (ICDs)

ICDs are the official, controlled documents that define every interface between two systems. Responsible Engineer: Systems Engineer (lead). Users: All subsystem engineers, Integration & Test, Launch Vehicle provider, Ground Segment team. ICDs eliminate ambiguity during integration — if two teams disagree about a pin assignment, protocol timing, or mechanical dimension, the ICD is the authority.

Types of ICDs
TypeBetween
Subsystem-to-subsystem ICDEvery pair of subsystems that share an interface (e.g., CDH ↔ EPS for power and data)

Spacecraft-to-ground ICDRF link parameters, modulation, coding, protocol, command & telemetry frame formats, ranging scheme
Internal ICDBetween units within a subsystem (e.g., OBC ↔ memory daughterboard, star tracker ↔ ADCS controller)

ICD Process
StepDescription
DraftDeveloped by the system engineer with input from both interface owners; identifies all interface points and parameters
ReviewReviewed and signed by responsible engineers from both sides of the interface

ICD Best Practices
  • Every interface parameter has a single owner responsible for its definition.
  • Use tables and diagrams — avoid prose descriptions of pin assignments or timing.
  • Define worst-case limits (min/max), not just nominal values.
  • Include verification method for each parameter (test, analysis, inspection).
  • Keep ICDs at the appropriate level of detail — enough for independent design, not a full schematic.
  • Use controlled distribution — everyone working on the interface must have the current version.

7. Fault Management

Fault management is the discipline of detecting, isolating, and recovering from spacecraft anomalies. It ensures that when something goes wrong — and something will — the spacecraft can protect itself, continue the mission if possible, or safen until ground intervention arrives. Fault management cross-cuts every other systems engineering function: budgets set the limits whose violation signals a fault, requirements specify fault tolerance and success criteria, ICDs define the signals used for fault detection, and V&V proves the fault responses work.

The Idea — FDIR & the Fault Management Lifecycle

FDIR (Fault Detection, Isolation, and Response/Recovery) is the core cycle. Every spacecraft fault follows this lifecycle:

StepWhat HappensExample
1. IdentificationDetermine what faults are credible based on failure modes, heritage, and analysisFMEA shows battery over-discharge is credible given regulator failure history
2. DetectionMonitor telemetry against thresholds and voting logic to recognize a fault has occurredBus voltage drops below 26 V for 3 consecutive 100 ms samples
3. IsolationContain the fault by disconnecting or powering down failed elements so the rest of the system continues safelyCommands EPS to isolate the failing battery string
4. ResponseExecute immediate corrective action — autonomous or ground-commanded — to restore safe stateTransition to safe-hold mode, orient sun-pointing, reduce loads
5. RecoveryReturn to nominal operations once the fault is resolved or worked aroundGround diagnoses root cause, uploads patch, re-enables payload operations

Fault analysis techniques used throughout the program:

TechniquePurpose
FTA (Fault Tree Analysis)Top-down deductive analysis — start from an undesired event (e.g., "loss of mission") and trace backward through gates to root causes. Good for system-level reliability analysis.
FMEA (Failure Modes and Effects Analysis)Bottom-up inductive analysis — for each component, list every failure mode, its effect on the subsystem and system, and existing mitigations. Generates a worksheet per component.
FMECAFMEA plus a criticality ranking for each failure mode (severity × probability). Focuses design effort on the highest-risk items.
Gap AnalysisCompare the faults identified by FTA/FMEA against the faults covered by the existing FDIR design. Gaps reveal missing detection logic or unhandled scenarios.
The Process — Fault Strategy, FDIR Layers & Design

Fault strategy defines the program's approach to fault management before any detailed design begins:

  • What fault tolerance level is required? (single-fault tolerant? double-fault tolerant?)
  • What autonomy level is expected? (ground-in-the-loop for all faults? autonomous recovery for critical faults?)
  • What success criteria drive the design? (mission success vs. spacecraft survival vs. safe state)
  • What heritage from previous missions can be reused?

FDIR layers operate at different levels of the hardware/software stack:

LayerScopeExamples
System-level FDIRDetects faults that affect the whole spacecraft — bus undervoltage, loss of attitude, communication blackoutSafe-hold mode entry, watchdog reset, solar array pointing recovery
Component-level FDIRDetects faults within a single subsystem unit — overtemp, overcurrent, sensor disagreementBattery heater stuck on → isolate heater; star tracker lost track → switch to backup
Board-level FDIRDetects faults on individual circuit cards — voltage rail faults, clock loss, SEU in RAMEDAC correction on memory read, watchdog timer expiry, voltage supervisor reset

FDIR design elements — how each fault is detected and handled:

ElementDescription
ThresholdThe telemetry value or limit that triggers detection — must be set above noise but below the point of damage. Typically defined as warning, alarm, and critical levels.
PersistenceHow long a threshold must be violated before a fault is declared. Prevents false trips from transient noise. Typical values: 3–10 consecutive samples.
Voting logicFor redundant sensors, how many must agree before a fault is declared. E.g., 2-of-3 voting for IMU redundancy.
Recovery actionWhat happens when the fault is confirmed. Options: log only, command retry, switch to redundant unit, enter safe hold, power cycle, call ground.
Fault loggingDetailed time-stamped record of the fault signature, sequence of events, and recovery outcome for post-event analysis.

Testing Fault Management

Fault management must be tested — it is not enough to document it on paper. The test program should include:

PhaseActivities
Unit-level injectionInject faults at the component level (e.g., disconnect a sensor, force a voltage rail out of spec) and verify the correct local detection and response. Includes WDT testing, EDAC error injection, and voltage supervisor threshold verification.
Subsystem-levelTest FDIR interactions within a subsystem — e.g., ADCS fault logic with redundant sensors, EPS load-shedding sequence, thermal heater failover. Verify that isolation correctly contains the fault.
System-levelEnd-to-end fault scenarios that cross subsystem boundaries. Example: simulate a battery failure and verify that the EPS shed loads, CDH entered safe hold, and COMMS switched to beacon mode — all autonomously.
Mission rehearsalRun nominal timeline with injected contingency events. Verify that FDIR responses do not interfere with critical mission events. Test ground-in-the-loop recovery procedures end-to-end.
Stress testingMultiple simultaneous faults, rapid fault sequences, worst-case fault scenarios (e.g., fault during a critical burn). Verify that the FDIR priority and pre-emption logic behaves correctly.

Fault management testing is often under-invested until late in the program. Early FDIR test planning — using hardware-in-the-loop or software-in-the-loop platforms — catches design gaps before hardware is built.
