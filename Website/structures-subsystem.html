Structures & Mechanisms Subsystem

The Structures & Mechanisms subsystem is the physical skeleton of the spacecraft. The structure provides the mechanical support to mount every component, withstands launch loads, and maintains alignment of sensitive instruments. Mechanisms enable deployments (solar arrays, antennas, booms), articulation (gimbals, pointing stages), and separation from the launch vehicle.

★ The Idea

The Structures & Mechanisms subsystem provides the mechanical foundation for everything on the spacecraft. It must be stiff enough to maintain alignment during maneuvers, strong enough to survive launch without yielding, light enough to fit the mass budget, and precise enough to ensure deployables work the first time — every time.

Every structural subsystem is defined by four core functions:

FunctionWhat it does
Primary StructureThe main load-bearing frame (bus panels, shear webs, longerons, thrust cone) that carries all loads from launch and on-orbit operations.
Secondary StructureBrackets, mounting plates, and struts that attach components (OBC, battery, reaction wheels, etc.) to the primary structure.
MechanismsMoving parts: hinge and deployment systems, separation systems, pointing gimbals, latch/release devices, and actuators.
Thermal/Mechanical InterfaceMounting surfaces that provide both mechanical attachment and thermal conduction paths (or isolation, as needed). Includes bondlines, washers, and interface fillers.

These functions are interdependent: primary structure design must accommodate all mechanisms and their loads, secondary structure must not couple dynamically with primary modes, and every interface must survive the vibration environment while maintaining alignment.

★ The Process

Responsible Engineers: Structural Engineer, Mechanical Engineer

Users: Systems Engineers, Integration & Test Engineers, Launch Vehicle Engineers, Manufacturing Engineers

Designing a structures & mechanisms subsystem begins with the launch vehicle interface — the loads it imposes, the envelope it provides, and the separation system it uses. Everything else follows from there.

The typical design workflow:

  1. Define the load environment — launcher-provided sine, random, acoustic, and shock levels at the spacecraft interface
  2. Choose the structural architecture — panel bus, central cylinder, truss, or monocoque based on mission class and mass budget
  3. Select materials — driven by stiffness, strength, thermal, and outgassing requirements
  4. Design the primary structure — size panels, rails, and load paths using FEM analysis
  5. Design mechanisms — deployment, separation, and articulation hardware with margin on all performance parameters
  6. Verify by test — modal survey, sine/random vibration, acoustic, shock, and mechanism life testing

A complete step-by-step walkthrough is in the Design Walkthrough — Worked Example below.

1. Structural Design

Structural design starts from the spacecraft layout — where each component sits, how mass is distributed, and where load paths run. The design must satisfy strength, stiffness, mass, and alignment requirements simultaneously.

Finite Element Modeling (FEM)

A FEM of the entire spacecraft is built to predict structural response under static, dynamic, and thermal loads. The model is correlated to test data (modal survey) and used to predict flight loads. Key outputs: modal frequencies and mode shapes, stress distribution, displacement under load, and interface loads at the launch vehicle attachment. Model correlation typically targets frequency agreement within 5% and mode shape cross-orthogonality > 0.95.

2. Loads & Environments

The design-driving loads come from the launch vehicle — vibration, acoustic, shock, and acceleration. On-orbit loads (thermal cycling, maneuvering, docking) are generally lower but drive fatigue and alignment stability.

Launch Loads
Load TypeDescription
Steady-state accelerationAxial (thrust) and lateral acceleration during launch; typically 3–10 g axial, 1–3 g lateral
Sine vibrationLow-frequency (5–100 Hz) structural response to engine thrust oscillation and wind gusts; typically 5–20 g at spacecraft interface
Random vibrationBroadband (20–2000 Hz) excitation from acoustic noise and aerodynamic buffeting; 5–15 g RMS typical
Acoustic noiseHigh sound pressure levels (130–150 dB) inside fairing at liftoff; drives large panel vibration and fatigue
ShockPyroshock from separation nuts, bolt cutters, and stage separations; > 1,000 g possible at 1–10 kHz; damages electronics and cracks ceramics
On-Orbit Environments
EnvironmentEffect on Structure
Thermal cycling±100 °C swings in LEO; differential thermal expansion causes bending and stress; cycle fatigue over 5,000–30,000 orbits
MicrogravityNo gravity sag; structures only loaded by inertia from maneuvering and thermal stress
VacuumOutgassing of adhesives and composites; cold welding of mechanisms; no convective cooling
Radiation & atomic oxygenDegradation of polymers, composites, and coatings; atomic oxygen erodes surfaces in LEO
Micrometeoroid & orbital debris (MMOD)Hypervelocity impacts (5–15 km/s); shielding needed for critical components; Whipple shields for pressurized elements
Load Cycle Derivation

The loads cycle starts with launcher-provided interface loads (sine, random, acoustic, shock). These are applied to the spacecraft FEM to predict component-level accelerations and stresses. Component design loads are typically derived by multiplying launcher input by a dynamic amplification factor, then applying safety factors. Coupled loads analysis (CLA) with the full launch vehicle + spacecraft FEM is performed for final verification.

3. Mechanisms

Materials Selection Considerations
  • Outgassing: Total mass loss (TML) < 1%, collected volatile condensable materials (CVCM) < 0.1% per ASTM E595.
  • Galvanic compatibility: Dissimilar metals in contact must be isolated (e.g., Al + CFC with insulating washer and wet-install primer).
  • CTE matching: Dissimilar CTE causes thermal stress and distortion; minimize mismatch in precision assemblies.
  • Radiation resistance: Polymers and composites degrade under radiation; TID tolerance must meet mission life.
  • Flammability: Materials must be self-extinguishing (NASA STD-6001) for crewed or habitable environments.

5. Structural Testing

Structural testing proves the design can survive the worst-case launch and on-orbit environments. Test levels are typically 1.25× (qualification) or 1.0× (acceptance) of predicted flight loads.

Test Sequence
TestPurpose
Modal surveyMeasure natural frequencies and mode shapes using low-level sine sweep or hammer impact; correlate FEM
Static load testApply ultimate design loads using whiffle tree or hydraulic actuators; verify no permanent deformation or rupture
Sine vibrationLow-frequency swept sine at qualification levels; verify no resonant amplification exceeds design allowables
Random vibrationBroadband random excitation; components and panels tested to acceptance or qualification levels; main workmanship test
Acoustic testLarge reverberant chamber at 130–150 dB; full spacecraft test; verifies large panel and appendage response
Shock testPyroshock simulation using resonant plate, tuned fixture, or live ordnance; verifies electronics and mechanical survivability
Mechanism functional testDeployment, articulation, and latch/release cycling at ambient and thermal vacuum conditions; life test to expected cycles + margin
Test Levels & Margins
Test TypeQualification LevelAcceptance Level
Sine vibration1.25× predicted max1.0× predicted max
Random vibration1.25× (or +3 dB)1.0×
Static load1.25× limit loadNot tested (verified by analysis + qual)
Shock1.25× predicted shock1.0×
Thermal vacuum±10 °C beyond predicted limits±5 °C beyond predicted limits
Model Correlation & Verification

After each structural test, the FEM is updated to match measured responses. Correlation targets: natural frequencies within 3–5%, mode shape cross-orthogonality > 0.9, and response amplitude within ±20% at critical locations. The correlated model is used to predict loads for flight conditions that can't be directly tested (e.g., coupled loads with launch vehicle). A verification closure report documents that all requirements are met with positive margin.

Step 3: Panel Thickness Sizing

The primary load for a cubesat is the quasi-static launch acceleration — typically 10 g axial, 3 g lateral per the launcher user's guide. For a 4 kg spacecraft, this gives a maximum axial load of 40 kgf (392 N).

The side panels (100 × 300 mm) are the primary load-bearing elements. A simply supported rectangular panel under lateral pressure has a maximum stress:

σmax = k × q × b² / t²

where k = 0.3 (for a/b ≈ 3), q = areal load, b = short side width, t = thickness. For a 2 mm Al 6061 panel:

First fundamental frequency of a panel:

f = (π/2) × √(E × t² / (12 × ρ × a⁴))

For Al 6061 (E = 69 GPa, ρ = 2,700 kg/m³), a 2 mm thick, 100 × 300 mm panel gives f ≈ 80 Hz — well above the typical 30 Hz minimum. For strength, a 1.5 mm panel also works but with less margin. Select 2 mm Al 6061-T6 panels as a conservative, low-risk choice.

Step 4: Structural Layout & Analysis Approach

The structural layout follows the standard 6U cubesat configuration:

  • Rails: Four aluminum 7075 rails at the corners, 8.5 × 8.5 mm cross-section, with the standard 85 mm rail-to-rail spacing. These transfer the entire launch load from the spacecraft to the dispenser.
  • Panels: Six 2 mm Al 6061-T6 panels (top, bottom, ±Y, ±Z) bolted to the rails. The bottom panel (Z−) serves as the interface to the separation system and contains the ejection springs.
  • Internal layout: Component stack along the Z-axis (longest dimension). The heaviest component (propulsion tank, 1.15 kg) is placed near the spacecraft center of mass to minimize bending moments.
  • FEM approach: Build a shell-element model in NASTRAN or equivalent. Apply 10 g axial + 3 g lateral simultaneously (combined load case) plus thermal loads (ΔT = ±50 °C for sun/shade transition). Check yield (1.25× safety factor) and ultimate (1.5×) margins. First mode should be > 30 Hz.
Step 5: Convergence

The preliminary structural design:

  • Chassis: 2 mm Al 6061-T6 panels, Al 7075-T73 rails
  • Tank: Ti-6Al-4V, 106 mm diameter sphere
  • Brackets: Al 6061-T6 CNC-machined
  • Fasteners: A286 stainless steel, #4-40 and #6-32
  • Total structural mass: ~0.55–0.75 kg (panels ~300 g, rails ~100 g, fasteners ~50 g, brackets ~100–250 g) — fits within the 680 g allocation

Now iterate:

  • Could 1.5 mm panels save 150 g of structural mass? A FEM analysis would confirm if the stiffness (first mode > 30 Hz) and strength margins are still positive. For a 4 kg spacecraft, 1.5 mm panels may be adequate.
  • Could carbon fiber composite panels save even more mass? CFC panels (1.5 mm, 0.5 mm faceskins on Al honeycomb) can cut panel mass by 40–50% but at 3–5× the cost. Worthwhile for mass-constrained missions.
  • What is the first mode frequency of the full stack? The component stack (propulsion tank + battery + OBC) has its own local modes. If any component mode couples with the primary structure mode (< 30 Hz), redesign the mounting bracket or add stiffeners.
  • Does the dispenser interface require specific rail tolerances? CubeSat standard requires rails flat to 0.1 mm over their length. If not met, the spacecraft may jam during ejection.

Each iteration tightens the design toward a structure that is stiff enough, strong enough, light enough, and compatible with the launcher and all other subsystems.
