Electrical Power Subsystem (EPS)

The Electrical Power Subsystem (EPS) generates, stores, regulates, and distributes electrical power to every other subsystem. It must supply reliable power through eclipse seasons, during peak loads, and over the full mission lifetime, with enough margin to handle degradation and contingency operations. Designing an EPS starts from the power profile — what each subsystem needs, when, and for how long — and works back through generation, storage, and distribution to close the budget.

Before You Dive In — The Big Picture

The EPS is the heartbeat of the spacecraft — if power fails, the mission is over. Every subsystem depends on a steady, regulated supply of power, and the EPS must deliver it through eclipses, peak loads, and degradation over years in orbit. The design starts with a power profile: a spreadsheet of what every component draws, in every mode, for every phase of the mission.

Every EPS is built from five functional blocks:

BlockWhat it does
Solar ArrayConverts sunlight into electrical power. Sizing must account for radiation degradation (EOL vs BOL), seasonal variation, and off-pointing.
BatteryStores energy for eclipse periods and peak loads. Chemistry, capacity, depth-of-discharge, and cycle life drive the design.
Power ConditioningRegulates the raw array/bus voltage to stable rails. DC-DC converters, voltage regulation, and bus filtering.
Power DistributionSwitches, fuses, and wiring that deliver power to each load with overcurrent protection and fault isolation.
Power ManagementHealth monitoring, MPPT (maximum power point tracking), load shedding, and battery management. The intelligence that keeps the system alive.

These blocks work together across every mission phase: the array charges the battery in sunlight, the battery powers the spacecraft in eclipse, and the distribution network delivers regulated power to every load — all while the power management system protects against faults and degradation. Section 6 ties everything together with a worked design example.

1. Power Generation

Solar arrays are the primary power source for nearly all spacecraft. Design starts with a power equipment list (PEL) and power profile — how much power the spacecraft needs and when. From there you determine the solar cell type, array configuration, and size needed.

Array Sizing — The Design Walkthrough

Solar array sizing follows a structured flow. For each decision factor, ask:

FactorWhat to ConsiderImpact on Design
1. Power profileWhat does each subsystem draw in each mode (sun-point, eclipse, payload, safe hold)? Sum the worst-case loads per mode.Determines the minimum power the array must deliver in sunlight and the battery must supply in eclipse.
2. Cell type selectionTriple-junction GaAs is the default for most missions. Silicon for low cost. Thin-film for flexibility. Multi-junction IMM for high efficiency.Higher efficiency cells reduce array area and mass but cost more. The efficiency directly drives array size.
3. Operational environmentWhere in space? LEO vs GEO vs interplanetary affects radiation dose, temperature range, and solar intensity (1/R² for interplanetary).Higher radiation means faster EOL degradation — over-size the array. Higher temperatures reduce cell voltage.
4. Mission lifetimeHow long must the array endure the environment? 1 year vs 5 years vs 15 years changes the total degradation.EOL power may be 70–85% of BOL. Longer missions need larger arrays at BOL to still meet requirements at EOL.
5. Sun angle / articulationBody-mounted (no articulation) gets cos(θ) solar incidence. Single-axis tracking keeps arrays normal to the sun within one axis. Full articulation keeps them always pointed at the sun.Body-mounted arrays in LEO can lose 30–50% of peak power due to angle. Articulation adds mass, complexity, and a mechanism (SADA) but delivers more power per unit area.
6. InefficienciesHow much of the substrate can be covered in cells? Battery recharge efficiency, MPPT losses, DC-DC conversion losses, harness IR losses.These accumulate. Typical end-to-end efficiency from array output to usable load power is 70–85%.
7. MarginDesign margin for uncertainty in degradation, power draw, and manufacturing tolerances.15–25% margin on generation at system level is typical.
Radioisotope Thermoelectric Generators (RTGs)

RTGs convert heat from radioactive decay into electricity using thermocouples (the Seebeck effect — a voltage generated by a temperature difference across dissimilar metals). RTGs are used when solar power is insufficient: deep space missions (> 2–3 AU from the Sun), long-duration polar missions, or missions that must survive without sunlight.

ParameterValue
EfficiencyVery low (~6%) but highly reliable (no moving parts)
FuelTypically Pu-238 (half-life = 87.7 years)
Power decayP(t) = P₀ × e^(−t / τ) where τ = t₁/₂ / ln(2)
AdvantagesContinuous power regardless of distance from Sun or eclipse; decades of operation
DisadvantagesHeavy; expensive; regulatory hurdles (nuclear launch authorization); limited Pu-238 supply
MPPT (Maximum Power Point Tracking)

Solar cells have a characteristic I-V curve where the maximum power point (P = V × I) occurs at a specific voltage. MPPT is the technique of operating the array at that voltage to extract the most power.

MethodComplexityEfficiencyNotes
Direct energy transfer (DET)Lowest70–85%Array connected directly to bus; excess power dissipated in shunt; simplest, most reliable
Perturb & observeLow85–95%Small voltage steps to find maximum power point; standard digital MPPT
Incremental conductanceMedium90–98%More accurate than P&O; good for rapidly changing illumination (eclipse exit)
Fractional open-circuit voltageLow80–90%Senses open-circuit voltage periodically; sets operating point at ~80% of Voc

2. Energy Storage

Batteries store energy for eclipse operation and peak loads. After the solar array is sized (determining how much charge current is available), the battery is sized to store enough energy to survive the longest eclipse plus margin. Chemistry, capacity, depth-of-discharge, and cycle life are the key design parameters.

Battery Chemistries
ChemistryEnergy Density (Wh/kg)Cycle LifeNotes
Li-ion (NCA, NMC)150–2505,000–20,000Most common; high density; needs careful charge management and cell balancing
LiFePO₄ (LFP)90–14015,000–30,000Lower density but very safe; longer cycle life; tolerant to overcharge
Li-S (emerging)350–500 (theoretical)100–1,000Very high energy density; short cycle life; R&D phase
NiCd (legacy)40–6020,000–50,000Very robust; memory effect; low density; heavy

Battery Design Considerations

When sizing a battery, these factors drive the design:

FactorConsiderations
SafetyLi-ion can enter thermal runaway if overcharged, over-discharged, or physically damaged. Cell selection, charge control, and mechanical design must prevent this.
Purpose & mission profileAre you powering through a 30-minute LEO eclipse or a 72-hour safe hold? The energy requirement determines total capacity.
Series vs parallel configurationSeries: increases total voltage while current stays the same. Used when components need higher voltage (e.g., reaction wheels at 28 V). Parallel: increases total current while voltage stays the same. Used for high peak-current draws (e.g., hold-down and release mechanisms for array deployment).
Cell balancingIn a series string, cells can drift apart. Balancing (passive or active) ensures all cells stay at the same state of charge.
Depth of dischargeLower DoD = longer cycle life. LEO batteries typically operate at 20–30% DoD for 30,000+ cycles. GEO batteries can operate at 60–80% DoD because they cycle only ~100 times per year (eclipse seasons).
Temperature rangeLi-ion typically operates at 0–40 °C. Outside this range, performance degrades and safety risks increase. Batteries are often the most temperature-sensitive component on the spacecraft.
Launch site handlingBatteries must be shipped, stored, and handled safely before launch. Some chemistries (Li-ion) have transport restrictions.
Fuel Cells (Alternative Power Source)

Fuel cells convert hydrogen and oxygen into electricity, water, and heat. They are used when the mission duration is short enough that cryogenic storage is feasible (hours to a few days), or when water is a useful byproduct.

ParameterValue
Reaction2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O + electricity + heat
Energy densityVery high (theoretically); but hydrogen and oxygen must be stored cryogenically (H₂ at −253 °C, O₂ at −183 °C)
Cell voltage~0.7 V per cell; stack voltage = number of cells × 0.7 V
Sizing math1 mole of H₂ = 2 g produces ~237 kJ = ~66 Wh. Required H₂ mass = E_req / 66 Wh/g (approximate)
ByproductsWater can be used for crewed missions or electrolyzed back into propellant; heat must be managed.
Key Battery Parameters
ParameterDefinition
Capacity (Ah)Total charge stored; usually specified at C-rate, temperature, and end-of-life
Depth of discharge (DoD)Percentage of capacity used per cycle; lower DoD = longer cycle life
State of charge (SoC)Current charge as percentage of capacity; tracked by coulomb counting + voltage
C-rateCharge/discharge rate relative to capacity; 1C = full discharge in 1 hour; LEO typically C/2 to C/5

3. Power Distribution

Power distribution delivers regulated voltage to every load, with overcurrent protection, fault isolation, and bus stability. The architecture choices here affect mass, reliability, and integration complexity.

Bus Architectures
ArchitectureDescriptionWhen Used
Unregulated busArray/battery voltage directly to loads; each load has its own regulatorSmall satellites, simple designs
Regulated busDC-DC converter maintains constant bus voltage; loads need less input filteringMost LEO/GEO spacecraft
Dual bus (regulated + unregulated)High-power loads on unregulated bus; sensitive electronics on regulated busMedium to large spacecraft
Distributed power architecturePoint-of-load converters near each load; reduces harness lossesHigh-power missions, modular designs
Voltage Levels
Bus VoltageCommon Uses

4. Power Budgeting

The power budget is the master spreadsheet that tracks generation vs consumption across all mission phases. It must close with margin at end of life, worst-case conditions.

Power Budget Elements
CategoryDescription
Power generation (solar array)Array power at BOL and EOL for minimum sun angle and worst-case temperature
Battery energy storageUsable energy at end-of-life, accounting for DoD limits and capacity degradation
Subsystem loadsAverage and peak power per subsystem per operating mode (sun-point, eclipse, payload, safe hold, thrusting)
Power bus lossesRegulator efficiency (80–95%), harness resistive losses, connector losses, distribution losses
Margin15–25% margin on generation, 10–15% on consumption; contingency reserve for uncertainty
Power Modes
ModeTypical Load (% of max)DurationNotes
Sun-point / safe hold40–60%IndefiniteAttitude control, heaters, basic COMMS; sun-pointing for max array power
Eclipse survival30–50%~30–60 min (LEO)Heaters, critical avionics only; battery supplies all power
Payload operation70–100%10–60 min per orbitInstruments, data handling, high-rate transmitter active

5. Power Management & Protection

The power management function keeps the EPS healthy — tracking the array's maximum power point, managing battery charge, protecting against faults, and autonomously shedding loads when energy is low.

Fault Protection
EventProtection
Battery overvoltageCharge termination at cell voltage limit; shunt regulator diverts excess array power
Battery undervoltageLoad shedding to prevent deep discharge; critical loads only below threshold
Overcurrent / short circuitSSPC or LCL trips; faulted load isolated; remaining bus continues operation
Bus undervoltageNon-critical loads shed; safe hold entry if voltage continues to drop
Cell imbalanceBalancing circuit equalizes cells; if imbalance persists, cell(s) may be unusable
Heater Power Management

Heaters can be the largest single power draw in eclipse. Management techniques include: proportional-integral control (PID) for precise temperature regulation, thermostat-controlled bang-bang heaters for simplicity, and duty-cycled heaters for power-constrained modes. Heater power should be included in the power budget with operational margins for cold-case scenarios.

6. EPS Design Process

Every spacecraft needs an Electrical Power Subsystem. The design follows a structured path from system-level requirements down to component selection, with trade-offs at every step. This section walks through the process and ends with a worked example.

Starting Requirements

EPS design begins with requirements derived from the mission and subsystem loads:

RequirementWhat It Defines
Power profileThe load in each operational mode (sun, eclipse, payload, safe hold) for every subsystem. Summed into orbit-average and peak power.
Bus voltage level28 V regulated is the standard for most spacecraft. Determines the DC-DC converter design and battery series configuration.
Cycling & chargingEclipse duration and frequency. Determines battery capacity, DoD, and charge rate. LEO: ~30 min eclipse, ~90 min orbit (15+ cycles/day). GEO: ~45 days eclipse season, 2×/year.
EPS component definitionBattery size, solar array EOL power, peak power handling, regulator type, distribution architecture.

Trade Spaces

System-Level Trades
TradeOptionsDrives
DC bus voltage5 V · 12 V · 28 V · 50 V · 120 VHigher voltage reduces harness losses but increases safety risk and component voltage ratings. 28 V is the heritage standard.
Power sourceSolar arrays · RTG · Fuel cells · Battery-onlyMission duration, distance from Sun, peak power requirement, regulatory burden (RTG needs nuclear launch approval).
Bus regulationUnregulated · Regulated · Dual busLoad sensitivity, cost, complexity. Unregulated is simplest but each load needs its own regulator.
Array articulationBody-mounted · Single-axis tracking · Full articulationPower requirement vs mass vs complexity vs pointing constraints. Body-mounted is simplest but delivers least power.
Component-Level Trades
ComponentOptionsKey Trade-off
Energy storageLi-ion · LiFePO₄ · NiCd · NiH₂ · Fuel cellEnergy density vs cycle life vs safety vs cost vs heritage
Charging methodCC-CV · Trickle charge · Pulse chargingCharge speed vs battery life vs complexity. CC-CV is standard for Li-ion.
Power conversionLinear regulator · Buck converter · Boost converter · SEPICEfficiency vs noise vs complexity vs cost. Switching converters are 80–95% efficient; linear regulators are simpler but waste power as heat.
Solar cell typeSi · Triple-junction GaAs · Multi-junction IMM · Thin-filmEfficiency vs cost vs rad tolerance vs mass vs flexibility
Battery configurationSeries (voltage) · Parallel (current) · Series-parallelVoltage requirement vs current requirement vs cell balancing complexity
EEE Part-Level Trades
TradeOptionsConsiderations
Radiation hardeningRad-hard by design · Rad-tolerant COTS · COTS with shieldingCost vs performance vs TID tolerance. COTS parts can be used in benign LEO orbits with shielding; deep space needs rad-hard components.
Parts qualitySpace-grade (QML V/B) · Military-grade · Automotive (AEC-Q100) · CommercialReliability vs cost vs availability. Battery cells in particular are often COTS automotive-grade with extensive screening.
Risk acceptanceSingle-string · Redundant (cold/hot spare) · FDIR-managedEPS reliability is mission-critical. Most spacecraft use redundant power paths and latch-up protected switching.

What Is Derating?

Derating is operating a component below its rated maximum to improve reliability. For the EPS, derating applies across every component:

ComponentDerating Example
Solar arrayDesign for EOL power at 70–85% of BOL; add 15–25% margin beyond worst-case load
BatteryLimit DoD to 20–30% (LEO) or 60–70% (GEO); derate capacity by 20% for EOL
DC-DC converterContinuous load ≤ 70% of rated output; input voltage range derated by 10% on each end
ConnectorsUse ≤ 60% of rated current per pin; derate temperature by 20 °C
Harness / wiringLimit current to ≤ 50% of rated ampacity for the wire gauge and insulation type
Power switches (SSPCs)Continuous load ≤ 70% of rated current; inrush handling within 80% of peak rating

Derating margins follow standards: ECSS-Q-ST-30-11C (European) and MIL-STD-975 (US). Higher-class missions require more conservative derating.

Typical Design Order

EPS design is a dependency chain — each decision constrains the next:

  1. Power profile & loads — Sum up every subsystem's power draw per mode. This is the starting point for everything.
  2. Bus voltage & architecture — Decide the bus voltage (28 V standard) and whether it's regulated, unregulated, or dual-bus.
  3. Solar array sizing — Determine the cell type, array configuration, and area to meet the power requirement at EOL with margin.
  4. Battery sizing — Once you know the array current and eclipse loads, size the battery for DoD, cycle life, and energy requirement.
  5. Power distribution — SSPCs, wiring, and connector layout. Driven by the number of loads, their locations, and their current draws.
  6. Power management & protection — MPPT algorithm, charge control, fault detection, load shedding thresholds. Designed to keep the system alive autonomously.
  7. Heater power allocation — Must fit within the eclipse budget. Often the largest unplanned load — model it early.

Worked Example: 6U Cubesat in LEO

Scenario & Power Profile

Mission: A 6U Earth observation cubesat at 500 km SSO. Payload camera draws 15 W for 10 minutes per orbit. COMMS transmitter draws 10 W during downlink (5-minute pass per orbit). OBC, ADCS, and heaters draw a baseline 8 W continuously.

ModeOBCADCSCOMMSPayloadHeatersTotal
Sun-point (baseline)2 W2 W1 W0 W3 W8 W
Payload operation3 W3 W1 W15 W2 W24 W
Downlink2 W2 W10 W0 W3 W17 W
Eclipse survival2 W2 W1 W0 W5 W10 W
Orbit Energy Calculation

At 500 km SSO, orbit period is ~94 minutes with ~35 minutes in eclipse. The orbit-average power and eclipse energy requirement:

PhaseDurationPowerEnergy
Sun-point baseline79 min (60 min baseline + transitions)8 W8 W × 3,540 s = 28.3 kJ
Payload operation10 min24 W24 W × 600 s = 14.4 kJ
Downlink5 min17 W17 W × 300 s = 5.1 kJ
Eclipse survival35 min10 W10 W × 2,100 s = 21.0 kJ
Total orbit energy94 min~68.8 kJ (sunlight: 47.8 kJ, eclipse: 21.0 kJ)

Eclipse energy = 21.0 kJ. Battery must supply this plus margin. With 20% margin: 25.2 kJ = 7.0 Wh.

Battery Sizing

Li-ion cells at 3.7 V nominal, 3 Ah each, 20% DoD per orbit:

  • Usable energy per cell per orbit: 3.7 V × 3 Ah × 20% DoD = 2.22 Wh
  • Cells needed: 7.0 Wh / 2.22 Wh = ~4 cells
  • Series configuration for bus voltage: 4 cells × 3.7 V = 14.8 V (boosted to 28 V bus via DC-DC)
  • Or 8 cells (4S2P) for margin: 4S = 14.8 V, 2P = 6 Ah total → 4.44 Wh usable per orbit at 20% DoD — plenty of margin

Solar Array Sizing

Sunlight energy needed: 47.8 kJ + 20% margin = 57.4 kJ. Plus battery recharge: 7.0 Wh × 1.2 (charge efficiency) = 8.4 Wh = 30.2 kJ. Total sunlight energy: ~87.6 kJ over 59 minutes of usable sunlit orbit time.

  • Required average array power: 87.6 kJ / 3,540 s = 24.7 W
  • With 30% margin on power (degradation, temperature, off-pointing): 24.7 W × 1.3 = 32.1 W
  • Triple-junction GaAs cells at 30% efficiency, 1,367 W/m² solar flux: need 32.1 / (1,367 × 0.30) = 0.078 m²
  • A 6U cubesat (~0.1 m² per face) can mount body-mounted cells on 1–2 faces, or use deployable panels for full coverage. With body-mount on 2 faces and cosine losses, you'd get ~0.12 m² effective — enough to meet the requirement.

Convergence

The preliminary numbers close: body-mounted triple-junction cells on two faces, a 4S2P Li-ion battery at 20% DoD, and a 28 V regulated bus. Now iterate: can you body-mount on 3 faces and reduce DoD to 15% for longer battery life? Can you use a single deployable panel instead for more power headroom? Each iteration tightens the design toward a converged EPS that meets the mission with comfortable margin.
