Guidance, Navigation & Control (GNC) Subsystem

The Guidance, Navigation & Control (GNC) subsystem — also called Attitude Determination and Control (ADCS) — determines the spacecraft's orientation and position in space and controls its attitude and trajectory. It keeps antennas pointed at Earth, solar arrays at the Sun, payloads at their targets, and propulsion burns in the right direction.

★ The Idea

The GNC subsystem is the spacecraft's sense of direction. It must know where it is (navigation), which way it's pointing (attitude determination), and how to change that (attitude control) — all without the visual cues we rely on Earth. GNC is the most mathematically intensive spacecraft subsystem, blending orbital mechanics, estimation theory, and feedback control.

The sections below give you the reference material — coordinate frames, sensor types, actuator sizing, control laws, and error budgets. But if you're designing a GNC system from scratch, start with the Design Walkthrough — it walks through a complete example (a 6U cubesat with a nadir-pointing Earth observation mission) step by step, showing how each decision flows from the mission requirements.

Every GNC subsystem is built from three fundamental capabilities:

CapabilityWhat it does
NavigationDetermines position and velocity in orbit. Uses GPS (in LEO), ground tracking, or onboard orbit propagation. Required for maneuver planning and timing.
Attitude DeterminationMeasures the spacecraft's orientation using sensors (star trackers, sun sensors, magnetometers, gyros). Sensor fusion via Kalman filters provides the best estimate.
Attitude ControlApplies torques to achieve and maintain the desired orientation. Uses actuators (reaction wheels, torque rods, thrusters) and a control law (PD, PID, LQR, etc.).

These three capabilities are tightly coupled: navigation provides position for sensor reference, sensors feed attitude estimates to the control law, and the control law commands actuators — all running at 1–100 Hz in the flight software.

★ The Process

Responsible Engineers: GNC/AOCS Engineer, Guidance & Control Engineer, Systems Engineer

Users: Systems Engineers, Flight Software Engineers, Operations Engineers, Payload Scientists

Designing a GNC subsystem begins with the mission's pointing requirements — what the spacecraft must point at, with what accuracy, and how fast it needs to move — and works through sensor selection, actuator sizing, control law design, and error budgeting.

The typical design workflow:

  1. Define the pointing requirements — accuracy, knowledge, stability, slew rate, and agility from the payload and mission needs
  2. Select the control architecture — three-axis stabilized vs spin-stabilized vs gravity-gradient; zero-momentum vs biased momentum
  3. Choose attitude sensors — star tracker, sun sensor, magnetometer, gyro, GPS; accuracy vs cost vs power trade
  4. Size the actuators — reaction wheels, torque rods, thrusters; momentum storage vs disturbance torques
  5. Design the control law — PD, PID, LQR, or sliding mode; gain tuning for stability and performance
  6. Build the pointing budget — allocate error among all contributors (sensor noise, alignment, thermal, jitter); verify RSS meets requirement
  7. Iterate and converge — revisit each trade as accuracy, mass, power, cost, and heritage constraints tighten

A complete step-by-step walkthrough is in the Design Walkthrough — Worked Example below.

1. Reference Frames & Orbit

GNC operates in multiple coordinate frames simultaneously. Understanding these frames and the spacecraft's orbit is essential for designing the determination and control algorithms.

ECEF (Earth-Centered Earth-Fixed)Earth centerRotates with Earth; used for GPS position, ground station pointing

Body frameSpacecraft CoMFixed to spacecraft structure; all sensors and actuators are defined in this frame

Attitude Representations
RepresentationParametersProperties
Euler angles (roll, pitch, yaw)3 anglesIntuitive; suffers from gimbal lock; used for visualization and small-angle control
Quaternions4 (scalar + vector)No singularities; computationally efficient; standard for onboard attitude estimation and control
Rotation matrix (DCM)3×3 matrix9 parameters, 6 constraints; used for transformations between frames; redundant but stable
Rodrigues parameters (Gibbs vector)3Singular at 180°; used in some attitude filters
Orbit Propagation

Onboard orbit propagation predicts the spacecraft's position for sensor pointing, ground contact, and eclipse entry/exit. Common propagators: SGP4 (simplified, for LEO), Keplerian two-body with J2 perturbation (most common onboard), and numerical integration (high accuracy, ground use). GPS provides direct position/velocity in LEO, which can be propagated forward between measurements.

2. Attitude Sensors

Sensors measure the spacecraft's orientation relative to known references — stars, the Sun, Earth's magnetic field, or inertial rate. Sensor fusion combines their outputs into a continuous attitude estimate.

Sensor Types
SensorAccuracyReferenceNotes
Star tracker1–30 arcsec (3σ)Star catalogMost accurate; works in eclipse; heavy processing; can be confused by Sun/Moon in FOV
Sun sensor (coarse / fine)0.1–5° (coarse), 0.01–0.1° (fine)Sun vectorWidely available; requires Sun in FOV; blind in eclipse
Magnetometer0.5–5°Earth's magnetic fieldLow cost, light; requires magnetic field model; degraded at high altitude (GEO); interference from spacecraft currents
Gyroscope (IMU)0.1–10 °/hr (bias stability)Inertial rateMeasures angular rate; integrates for attitude between absolute sensor updates; drift accumulates over time
GPS receiver~10 m position, ~0.1 m/s velocityGPS constellationProvides position, velocity, and precise timing (PPS); limited to LEO/MEO; requires 4+ satellites in view
Earth horizon sensor0.1–0.5°Earth IR horizonUsed on heritage missions; measures Earth disk center; heavy, being replaced by star trackers
Sensor Fusion (Kalman Filter)

A Kalman filter combines measurements from multiple sensors with a dynamic model (Euler's equations for rotation) to produce the best estimate of attitude and rate. The filter predicts forward using the dynamics, then corrects using sensor measurements. Typical architecture: gyros propagate attitude at high rate (10–100 Hz), star tracker/sun sensor updates correct gyro drift at lower rate (1–10 Hz). Extended Kalman filters (EKF) handle the nonlinear attitude dynamics.

Sensor Alignment & Calibration
ActivityPurpose
Boresight alignmentMeasure the fixed rotation between each sensor frame and the body frame; typically 0.01–0.1° accuracy needed
Gyro calibrationDetermine bias, scale factor, and misalignment; done on-orbit through rotation maneuvers or star tracker updates
Magnetometer calibrationRemove hard-iron and soft-iron biases from spacecraft magnetic fields; requires on-orbit sphereing maneuver
Star tracker alignment monitorMonitor boresight shift due to thermal distortion over orbit; may need periodic updates

3. Attitude Actuators

Actuators apply torque to the spacecraft to change or maintain its orientation. The choice of actuator depends on the torque magnitude, duty cycle, pointing accuracy, and power available.

Actuator Types
ActuatorTorquePowerNotes
Reaction wheel0.01–2 N·m1–100 W (peak)Most common for fine pointing; momentum exchange device, not propellant-limited; but can saturate (needs desaturation via torque rods or thrusters)
Torque rod (magnetorquer)0.001–0.5 N·m0.1–10 WTorques against Earth's magnetic field; no propellant; limited to LEO (field is weak); used for momentum management and detumbling
Control moment gyro (CMG)10–5,000+ N·m10–500 WHigh torque; used on large spacecraft (ISS, Hubble); complex, heavy, expensive
Thruster (ACS thrusters)0.1–100 NPropellantProvides both torque and translation; uses propellant; minimum impulse bit limits fine pointing; used for safe hold, large maneuvers
Reaction control system (RCS) thrusters1–500 NPropellantPair of thrusters in opposite directions for pure torque; used for station-keeping and momentum desaturation
Reaction Wheel Management
IssueDescription
Momentum saturationWheels can only store so much momentum before reaching maximum speed. External torques (gravity gradient, solar pressure, magnetic) accumulate over time. Desaturation (momentum dumping) uses torque rods or thrusters to remove excess momentum.
Zero-speed crossingWhen a wheel passes through zero RPM, static friction (stiction) causes torque ripple and pointing jitter. Mitigations: biased momentum (wheels always spin above a minimum speed) or null-space management for 4+ wheel configurations.
Vibration and jitterWheel imbalance (static and dynamic) generates micro-vibration that degrades pointing for sensitive payloads. Isolators and balanced wheels reduce jitter.
Lifetime / bearingsWheel bearings have finite life (5–15+ years depending on speed and load). Lubricant degradation is the primary failure mode.
Actuator Sizing

Reaction wheel sizing: maximum angular momentum (N·m·s) must exceed worst-case disturbance torque × desaturation interval. Torque rod sizing: dipole moment (A·m²) must produce enough torque in the weakest magnetic field at your orbit. Thruster sizing: thrust level must provide adequate acceleration for the maneuver within the desired burn duration.

4. Control Methods

The control law determines how measured attitude error is translated into actuator commands. The choice of control method depends on pointing accuracy, agility requirements, and the mission phase.

Gravity-gradient1–10°None (passive)Very lowEarly LEO missions; uses gravity gradient boom for passive nadir pointing
Three-axis (zero momentum)0.001–0.5°HighHighMost modern spacecraft; separate control of each axis; fast slewing; needs active control in all axes
Three-axis (biased momentum)0.01–1°ModerateMediumOne wheel provides gyroscopic stiffness in one axis; simpler than zero-momentum but less agile
Dual-spin0.01–0.5°ModerateMediumSpinning section (rotor) provides stability; despun section points payload; used on some GEO comsats

5. Pointing & Navigation Budgets

Budgets allocate allowable error among all contributors — sensor noise, actuator resolution, structural misalignment, thermal distortion, and control law performance. The total must meet the mission's pointing and navigation requirements.

Pointing Budget
Error SourceTypical Contribution
Sensor noise / accuracyStar tracker noise: 1–30 arcsec; gyro drift: 0.1–10 °/hr
Sensor misalignmentBoresight alignment uncertainty: 10–100 arcsec; thermal distortion: 10–200 arcsec
Actuator resolutionWheel torque ripple; thruster minimum impulse bit: 0.01–1 mN·s
Control law errorSteady-state tracking error; limit cycle amplitude: 0.1–100 arcsec
Structural dynamicsSolar array / boom vibration; flexible modes excited by wheel or thruster disturbances: 1–50 arcsec
Thermal distortionSun/shade transition causes structure to bend: 10–500 arcsec (worst case for large appendages)
Total (RSS)Root-sum-square of all contributors; must be less than requirement (e.g., 0.05° = 180 arcsec)

Design Walkthrough — Worked Example

This walkthrough selects sensors and sizes actuators for a 6U cubesat with a nadir-pointing Earth observation mission, following the design-thinking process used throughout this site.

Step 1: Mission Requirements

Scenario: 6U Earth observation cubesat at 500 km SSO. Requirements derived from the payload (multi-spectral camera, 10 m GSD, 10 km swath):

ParameterRequirementDrives
Pointing accuracy≤ 0.1° (360 arcsec) 3σNeed star tracker or fine sun sensor + Kalman filter
Pointing knowledge≤ 0.05° (180 arcsec) 3σDetermines geolocation accuracy of imagery
Pointing stability (jitter)≤ 10 arcsec over 10 ms exposureReaction wheel balancing, vibration isolation
Slew rate≥ 1°/sAgility for target acquisition and downlink pointing
Control modeThree-axis stabilized, nadir-pointingStandard for Earth observation
Mission life3 yearsWheel bearing life, sensor degradation

The driving requirement is pointing knowledge ≤ 0.05°, which demands a star tracker for absolute attitude reference.

Step 2: Sensor Selection

For a 6U cubesat, sensor selection balances accuracy against size, power, and cost:

SensorSelected TypeAccuracyPowerMassRationale
Star trackerMicro star tracker (e.g., Blue Canyon, CubeSTAR)5–20 arcsec0.5–2 W~100 gProvides absolute attitude reference; meets 0.05° knowledge requirement with margin
Sun sensorFine sun sensor (2-axis)0.1°0.1 W~10 gBackup for star tracker; used in safe hold and sun acquisition
Magnetometer3-axis fluxgate0.5° (with IGRF model)0.1 W~20 gRate damping, detumble, backup attitude in eclipse
Gyro (IMU)MEMS gyro (e.g., ADIS, STIM300)0.5–5 °/hr bias0.5–1 W~30 gRate sensing for control law; propagates attitude between star tracker updates
GPS receiverSpaceborne GPS (e.g., NovAtel, SkyFox)~5 m position0.5–1 W~50 gPosition and velocity for orbit propagation and timing

Total sensor mass: ~210 g. Total sensor power: ~2–4 W (continuous, with star tracker operating intermittently at 1 Hz).

Step 3: Actuator Sizing

The primary disturbance torque for a 6U at 500 km is gravity gradient (~1–5 µN·m), followed by solar pressure (~0.1–0.5 µN·m) and residual magnetic (~0.1–1 µN·m). Worst-case total disturbance: ~5 µN·m.

For reaction wheels, the key sizing parameter is momentum storage. With a 5 µN·m disturbance and a 90-minute orbit (5,400 s), the accumulated momentum per orbit is:

h = τ × t = 5 × 10⁻⁶ × 5,400 = 0.027 N·m·s per axis per orbit

With desaturation every 1 orbit via magnetorquers, each wheel needs at least 0.03 N·m·s capacity. Small reaction wheels (e.g., Sinclair RW-0.03) provide 0.03 N·m·s — exactly at the limit. For margin, select wheels with 0.06 N·m·s capacity. Four wheels in a pyramid configuration provide redundancy and momentum management flexibility.

Magnetorquers (torque rods) are sized to desaturate the wheels within one orbit. Required dipole moment:

m = τ / B = 5 × 10⁻⁶ / 25 × 10⁻⁶ ≈ 0.2 A·m²

where B ≈ 25 µT is the magnetic field strength at 500 km. Torque rods with 0.5 A·m² provide margin for faster desaturation. Three orthogonal rods (one per axis) provide full 3-axis control.

Step 4: Pointing Budget & Control Mode

The pointing budget allocates error among all contributors, root-sum-square (RSS):

Error SourceAllocation (arcsec, 3σ)
Star tracker noise20
Star tracker alignment uncertainty30
Gyro drift between updates40
Thermal distortion (sun/shade)50
Control law error (limit cycle)30
Reaction wheel jitter10
Total RSS~80 arcsec (0.022°)

The total ~80 arcsec is well within the 180 arcsec (0.05°) knowledge requirement and the 360 arcsec (0.1°) accuracy requirement. The design has margin to absorb additional unmodeled errors.

Control architecture: Three-axis zero-momentum, PD control law with quaternion error feedback. Reaction wheels at 10 Hz control rate, Kalman filter at 1 Hz (star tracker updates), gyro propagation at 100 Hz between updates. Magnetorquers for wheel desaturation once per orbit (B-dot or cross-product law).

Step 5: Convergence

The preliminary GNC design:

  • Sensors: Micro star tracker + fine sun sensor + 3-axis magnetometer + MEMS gyro + GPS
  • Actuators: 4 × 0.06 N·m·s reaction wheels (pyramid), 3 × 0.5 A·m² torque rods
  • Control: PD quaternion feedback, 10 Hz control rate, Kalman filter at 1 Hz
  • Total GNC mass: ~0.6 kg (sensors 210 g + wheels 300 g + rods 100 g)
  • Total GNC power: ~3–5 W (continuous operation)

Now iterate:

  • Could a higher-performance star tracker (5 arcsec) relax the alignment and thermal distortion budgets? If the tracker is more accurate, maybe the thermal budget can be looser, reducing structure cost.
  • Could wheel desaturation be done less frequently (every 5 orbits) by using larger wheels (0.15 N·m·s)? Larger wheels add mass but reduce magnetorquer duty cycle.
  • Is GPS necessary? If orbit determination from ground tracking is sufficient (1 km position knowledge), the GPS can be removed, saving 50 g and 0.5 W.
  • Can the pointing accuracy requirement be relaxed to 0.5° (typical for non-imaging missions)? Then a star tracker is not needed — sun sensor + magnetometer + gyro would suffice, saving significant cost.

Each iteration tightens the design. The converged answer depends on the real mass, power, and cost margins available.
