Thermal Control Subsystem

The Thermal Control subsystem maintains all spacecraft components within their allowable temperature ranges throughout the mission. In the vacuum of space, the only heat transfer mechanisms are radiation and conduction — there is no convection. The thermal design must simultaneously reject excess heat from electronics and absorb enough heat to keep cold components (batteries, propellant lines) above their minimum temperatures.

★ The Fundamentals

The Thermal subsystem is the spacecraft's skin and metabolism — it controls how heat flows in and out. Without it, electronics would overheat in the sun and freeze in eclipse, propellant lines would burst, and batteries would fail. Thermal design is always a balancing act between rejecting waste heat and retaining enough warmth during cold periods.

Every thermal control system uses five fundamental techniques:

TechniqueWhat it does
Surface coatingsControl solar absorptance (α) and infrared emittance (ε). The α/ε ratio determines whether a surface heats up or cools down in sunlight.
Multi-layer insulation (MLI)Blankets of alternating reflective and spacer layers that reduce radiative heat transfer to/from the spacecraft.
RadiatorsSurfaces designed to reject heat to space. Sized to dissipate the maximum waste heat from electronics and payloads.
Heat pipesPassive devices that transport heat from hot components to radiators using phase-change (evaporation/condensation).
Heaters & coolersActive elements: electrical heaters warm cold components; thermoelectric coolers (TECs) or mechanical cryocoolers provide active refrigeration.

These techniques are combined into a thermal design that balances every heat source (electronics, Sun, Earth IR, albedo) against every heat sink (space at 2.7 K, radiator surfaces) across all mission phases.

1. Thermal Environment

The space environment is the driving input to thermal design. Unlike on Earth, heat is exchanged only through radiation, and the boundary conditions vary dramatically between sunlight and shadow, and with orbit type and attitude.

2. Heat Transfer Fundamentals

Thermal design is built on the physics of heat transfer. In space, conduction and radiation dominate; convection is absent. Understanding these mechanisms is essential for sizing radiators, heat pipes, and insulation.

Conduction

Heat flow through solid materials: Q = k·A·ΔT / L, where k is thermal conductivity (W/m·K), A is cross-sectional area, ΔT is temperature difference, L is path length. Key materials: aluminum (~200 W/m·K) for heat spreading; copper (~400 W/m·K) for high-flux straps; pyrolytic graphite (up to 1,500+ W/m·K in-plane) for space-limited paths. Thermal interface materials (TIMs) like gap fillers or thermal greases reduce contact resistance between bolted joints.

Radiation

Heat radiated from a surface: Q = ε·σ·A·(T⁴ − Tspace⁴), where ε is emittance, σ = 5.67×10⁻⁸ W/m²·K⁴ is Stefan-Boltzmann constant, T is surface temperature. Radiated power scales with T⁴ — a small temperature increase greatly increases heat rejection. Surface finish dominates radiative properties: white paint (α~0.2, ε~0.9) stays cool; black paint (α~0.9, ε~0.9) absorbs heat; silvered Teflon (α~0.1, ε~0.8) is used for radiators.

3. Passive Thermal Control

Passive thermal control requires no power or moving parts. It relies on surface finishes, insulation, conduction paths, and phase-change materials to maintain temperatures within limits.

Surface Finishes & Coatings

Multi-Layer Insulation (MLI)

MLI blankets consist of alternating layers of reflective material (aluminized Kapton or Mylar) separated by low-conductivity netting (Dacron or Nomex). A typical 10–20 layer blanket has an effective emittance of 0.005–0.02. MLI covers most of the spacecraft exterior to minimize heat loss to space in eclipse and heat gain from the Sun in sunlight. Cutouts are provided for radiators, sensors, antennas, and thermal interfaces.

Radiator Sizing

Radiator area required: A = Q / (ε·σ·(T⁴ − Tspace⁴)). For typical electronics at 40 °C: ~0.03–0.05 m² per 10 W rejected. Radiators face away from the Sun (usually the anti-nadir or anti-velocity face in LEO) and are coated with low-α/high-ε finishes. Variable-emittance radiators can passively adjust heat rejection as temperature changes. Radiator must be over-sized for EOL when degradation increases α.

Heat Pipes
TypeWorking FluidTemp RangePower Capacity

Phase-Change Materials (PCMs)

PCMs absorb heat during melting and release it during freezing, acting as thermal flywheels. Common materials: paraffin wax (latent heat ~200 J/g, melt point 20–70 °C) and salt hydrates. Used to buffer transient heat loads or maintain components above minimum temperature during eclipse.

4. Active Thermal Control

Active thermal control uses electrical power to heat or cool components. It provides precise temperature regulation but consumes power and adds mass.

5. Thermal Analysis & Testing

Thermal design is verified through analysis models (finite element/difference thermal math models) and environmental testing in thermal vacuum chambers. Correlation between model and test is essential for flight confidence.

Thermal Analysis
Analysis TypePurpose
Steady-state analysisPredicts equilibrium temperatures for each operational mode (hot case, cold case, survival mode)
Transient analysisPredicts temperature evolution through orbit: sun → eclipse → sun transitions; heater cycling
Hot / cold case definitionWorst-case hot: max solar flux, min spacecraft age (best coatings), max internal dissipation. Cold case: min solar flux, max age, min dissipation
Margin philosophyAnalysis typically includes 5–20 °C margin on predictions; component qualification limits include margin beyond allowable flight temperature
De-orbiting / end-of-lifeThermal analysis for disposal phase: propellant freezing, battery thermal management after passivation
Thermal Vacuum (TVAC) Testing
Test TypeDescription
Thermal balanceStabilize at hot and cold cases; measure temperature distribution; correlate with thermal model
Thermal cyclingCycle through predicted temperature extremes (typically 4–12 cycles) for workmanship and margin verification
Burn-in / life testExtended hot operation (72–168 hours) to identify infant mortality in electronics
Survival / non-operatingVerify components survive worst-case non-operating temperatures without damage
Model Correlation

A thermal math model (TMM) is built before testing, then updated to match test data by adjusting uncertain parameters (contact conductances, coating properties, MLI effectiveness). Correlation targets: within ±3–5 °C for most nodes, ±2 °C for critical components. The correlated model becomes the flight prediction tool for on-orbit conditions.

★ The Process

Responsible Engineers: Thermal Engineer, Systems Engineer, Mechanical Engineer

Users: Systems Engineers, Mechanical Engineers, Electronics Engineers, Operations Engineers

Designing a thermal control subsystem begins with understanding the thermal environment — how much heat the spacecraft receives from the Sun, Earth, and its own electronics, and how much it loses to the cold of space. The thermal design must keep every component within its allowable temperature range across all mission phases.

The typical design workflow:

  1. Define the thermal environment — orbit type, Sun/Earth heat loads, eclipse duration, beta angle extremes
  2. Quantify internal dissipation — power draw of every component, duty cycles, hot/cold case power modes
  3. Choose passive control approach — surface coatings, MLI coverage, radiator sizing, heat pipe routing
  4. Add active control where needed — heater power for cold cases, thermoelectric coolers for sensitive instruments
  5. Build and correlate the thermal model — finite-element model, hot/cold case analysis, TVAC test correlation
  6. Iterate and converge — adjust radiator area, heater setpoints, and MLI coverage as analysis reveals hot or cold spots

A complete step-by-step walkthrough is in the Design Walkthrough — Worked Example below.

Design Walkthrough — Worked Example

This walkthrough sizes a passive thermal control system for a 6U cubesat in LEO using the same design-thinking approach as the other subsystems.

Step 1: Mission Requirements & Thermal Environment

Scenario: 6U Earth observation cubesat at 500 km SSO, 94-minute orbit (35 min eclipse). Components have the following dissipation and temperature limits:

ComponentDissipation (W)Operating Range (°C)Survival Range (°C)
OBC2−20 to +70−30 to +85
COMMS transceiver10 (peak)−10 to +55−20 to +70
Payload camera15 (peak)+10 to +40−10 to +60
Battery (Li-ion)0 (passive)+5 to +35−5 to +45
ADCS (reaction wheels)2−10 to +50−20 to +65
GPS receiver0.5−20 to +60−30 to +75

The most restrictive component is the battery (+5 to +35 °C operating), which drives the need for both heating (in eclipse) and cooling (in sunlight).

Step 2: Heat Balance — Hot Case

Worst-case hot: max internal dissipation, Sun at normal incidence, beginning of life (best coating α/ε). Average internal dissipation during sunlight operations: ~15 W (baseline 8 W + intermittent payload/comms).

The spacecraft is MLI-covered except for one radiator face. Solar input on the non-MLI radiator is minimized by pointing it away from the Sun (anti-nadir face in LEO). The radiator sees only Earth IR (~240 W/m²) and albedo (~150 W/m² reflected).

Heat must be rejected from the radiator by radiation to space (2.7 K). The radiator is coated with silver Teflon (α = 0.12, ε = 0.82). Assuming the radiator is at 35 °C (308 K) at the hot case:

Qrad = ε·σ·A·(T⁴ − Tspace⁴) − α·Gabsorbed

where Gabsorbed is the absorbed environmental flux (Earth IR + albedo) on the radiator face. For a first pass, assume the environmental absorption approximately cancels with parasitic heat leaks, so Qrad ≈ ε·σ·A·T⁴.

Step 3: Radiator Sizing

To reject 15 W internal dissipation with the radiator at 35 °C (308 K) and silver Teflon coating (ε = 0.82):

A = Q / (ε·σ·T⁴) = 15 / (0.82 × 5.67×10⁻⁸ × 308⁴)

= 15 / (0.82 × 5.67×10⁻⁸ × 9.00×10⁹) = 15 / 418 = 0.036 m²

A 6U cubesat has faces of ~0.06 m² (10 × 30 cm = 0.03 m² per face, so two adjacent faces). A 0.036 m² radiator fits comfortably on a single 10 × 30 cm face (0.03 m²) with 20% margin, or across two partial faces. In practice, dedicate one full 10 × 30 cm face as the radiator (~0.03 m²) and use conductive strapping to spread heat to it.

Step 4: Heater Power — Cold Case

Worst-case cold: eclipse, minimum internal dissipation (~8 W from OBC + ADCS), end of life (coating degradation increases α, but in eclipse there's no solar input). The spacecraft loses heat through MLI and the radiator. MLI effective emittance is ~0.02; assume the non-radiator area (~0.5 m² for a 6U) loses heat at a rate:

QMLI = εeff·σ·A·(T⁴ − Tspace⁴) ≈ 0.02 × 5.67×10⁻⁸ × 0.5 × (293⁴ − 3⁴) ≈ 4.2 W

The radiator (ε = 0.82, A = 0.03 m²) loses:

Qrad = 0.82 × 5.67×10⁻⁸ × 0.03 × (293⁴ − 3⁴) ≈ 10.3 W

Total heat loss in eclipse ≈ 14.5 W. Internal dissipation ≈ 8 W. Shortfall = ~6.5 W — this is the heater power needed to maintain battery temperature above +5 °C.

A 7 W heater on the battery with a thermostat set at +7 °C (turn-on) / +12 °C (turn-off) provides margin. Over 35 min eclipse: energy = 7 W × 35 min = 7 × 2,100 s = 14.7 kJ = 4.1 Wh. This must be included in the EPS eclipse energy budget.

Step 5: Convergence & Design Summary

The preliminary thermal design:

  • Radiator: One 10 × 30 cm face, silver Teflon coating, conducting straps from OBC and COMMS to radiator
  • MLI: 15-layer Kapton/Nomex on all non-radiator exterior faces
  • Heater: 7 W Kapton-foil patch on battery, thermostat-controlled at 7/12 °C
  • Thermal strap: Copper braid from camera to structure for heat spreading

Now iterate:

  • Can the radiator be reduced if the payload dissipates only 15 W for 10 minutes? The transient thermal mass of the structure (~900 J/kg·K for Al) buffers short peaks. A transient analysis might show the radiator can be 20% smaller.
  • Could a variable-emittance radiator reduce heater power by rejecting less heat in the cold case?
  • What if the battery survival temperature is −5 °C (as some Li-ion cells allow)? Then heater power drops to near zero — the 8 W internal dissipation may be sufficient.
  • If the orbit had a higher beta angle (less eclipse), the cold case improves and heater power reduces.

Each iteration tightens the design toward a thermal control system that keeps every component within its temperature range with minimum mass and power.
